# 2 – Home
**POV: Becky**

---

The blinds are closed, but the Alabama heat still finds a way in. It presses against the glass, thick and wet, making the air inside the living room feel like a warm damp towel. The AC is humming a low, rattling tune, fighting a losing battle against the mid-afternoon humidity. It smells like stale cigarettes and the kind of old upholstery that absorbs every failure the house has ever seen.

I'm sprawled on the floor, leaning against the base of the couch. I know exactly which floorboard doesn't creak and which drawer in the kitchen holds the half-empty bags of pretzels. I reach up and snag a handful of chips from the bowl on the coffee table without looking.

Alex is hunched over her device. It's an old model, a slim piece of glass with a spiderweb crack running from the top left corner down through the center. She holds it with both hands, her thumbs moving in a rhythmic, desperate scroll.

"Coastal Scrubbing and Remediation," she reads aloud. Her voice is flat. "Entry-level technician. Must be able to operate a basic filtration drone. No experience required."

"Apply," I say, chewing. "You can fly a drone. You flew that one into the creek in eighth grade."

"That was a toy, Becky."

"Still a drone."

She taps the screen. The crack in the glass catches the dim light, splitting the image of the job posting into two jagged halves. She spends a few minutes typing, her slight frame curled into a ball, making her look even smaller than she is.

Across the room, her mom's boyfriend is sitting in the oversized recliner. He's a large man who seems to take up more than his fair share of the room's oxygen. He isn't talking. He isn't even looking at us. His eyes are wide, tracking something invisible across the ceiling. He follows a slow, drifting arc from the corner of the room toward the light fixture, his pupils dilated. Every few seconds, his gaze jumps—a sudden, sharp flick to the left, as if a spark just went off in the air. He's deep in the Glitter. He's seeing stars in a room with no windows.

Alex doesn't look at him. She just keeps scrolling.

The device pings. A short, sharp sound that cuts through the AC's rattle.

Alex freezes. She stares at the screen for a long time. Then she lets out a breath, a slow exhale that doesn't quite clear her lungs.

"What is it?" I ask.

"Data Tagging Inc.," she says. "The remote moderation gig. They moved forward with other candidates."

"Algorithmic rejection," I say. "Probably didn't like your font."

She doesn't laugh. She just scrolls again. Her hand goes to her neck, fingers curling around the dog tag pendant. She tugs on the chain, pulling the metal disc a few inches away from her skin and holding it there for a second before letting it drop back against her chest.

The sound of footsteps comes from the hallway. Mom walks through the room, carrying a stack of mail and a phone pressed to her ear. She looks tired. Not the kind of tired that goes away with a nap, but the kind that settles into the skin, making the corners of her mouth droop. She's wearing an oversized t-shirt from a high school volleyball team from twenty years ago.

She stops for a second, looking at Alex.

"Did you check the mail?" Mom asks. She doesn't wait for an answer, her attention already drifting back to the phone call. "I know, I know. I'll call the office back on Monday. Just leave a message."

She looks at Alex again, a brief, distant flicker of a smile. "You look pale, honey. Maybe eat something."

Then she's gone, disappearing back into the hallway to finish her conversation. Her voice floats back to us, something about a disputed utility bill, the tone bored and exhausted.

Her mom's boyfriend makes a small noise in the back of his throat, a soft, humming sound. He's still watching the ceiling. He shifts his weight, and the recliner groans under him, a heavy, metallic sound that fills the silence.

Alex is back to the scrolling. She finds another one. "Warehouse Logistics. Night shift. Sorting hub."

"That's a lot of walking," I say.

"It's a paycheck," she says.

She starts typing again. The room is dim, the shadows stretching long across the worn carpet. Outside, a car horn blares somewhere down the street, a lonely sound in the heat.

I reach for another chip. The bowl is almost empty. I watch the way Alex's shoulder blades poke through her thin shirt as she leans further into the screen, her eyes fixed on the flickering glass.

The AC kicks into a higher gear, rattling the vent in the ceiling. A thin layer of dust shakes loose and drifts down through the air, illuminated by a single stray beam of light that managed to slip through the blinds. It lands on the coffee table and stays there.
