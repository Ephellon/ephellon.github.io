# 4a – Smoke Shop (Becky's)
**POV: Becky**

---

The humidity is a wet blanket draped over the neighborhood. Golden hour in Blueville isn't a glow; it's a thick, yellow haze that smells of salt and exhaust. Alex is walking half a pace behind me, her voice thin and shaking. She's still talking about the money—the two thousand dollars her mother's boyfriend took—and how her mother looked her in the eye and called her a thief.

Alex keeps twisting the dog tag pendant around her neck. Her fingers are pale, her movements jerky. She looks smaller than usual in the oversized band tee, her short brown hair sticking up in messy tufts from the damp air.

"She just... she actually believes him," Alex says. "She thinks I'm the one who—"

"Stop," I say. I don't stop walking. "Don't do the loop, Al. The loop doesn't get the money back."

She goes quiet, but her breathing is fast. I can see the grey of her eyes darting around, looking for a place to land. She has nowhere to go. I can see it in the way she's clutching the strap of her bag, like it's the only thing keeping her upright.

"I know a place," I tell her. "Somewhere we can figure this out. Someone who can actually help."

She looks at me. There's that look—the one she's had since seventh grade. Total, unblinking trust. It's the kind of look that makes me feel like I could tell her the sky is neon green and she'd start looking for a marker to paint it. I reach back and grab her wrist, pulling her along.

"Come on. It's an easy fix."

We hit the main drag three blocks later. The Smoke Shop sits on a corner that feels like it's been decaying since the nineties. The exterior signage is a faded, peeling yellow that looks like a bruised banana. A blue neon "OPEN" sign hums in the window, casting a jittery light onto the sidewalk, and the red awning above the door is bleached almost white by the Alabama sun.

Inside, the air is cooler, smelling of cheap incense and stale tobacco. The lighting is a mix of those same colors—yellow Glitter advertisements taped to the glass, blue neon tubes running along the ceiling, and red packaging for various consumables lining the shelves.

Q is behind the counter. He's leaning on a rag, slowly polishing a glass display case. He doesn't look up as the bell rings, but he knows we're there. When he finally lifts his head, he gives me a short, slow nod. It's a recognition of territory. He doesn't look at Alex. He doesn't nod. He just looks past her, as if she's a piece of furniture I've brought along for the ride.

"Hey, Q," I say.

He doesn't answer. He just gestures with a chin-flick toward the back booth, the one tucked behind a rack of oversized novelty lighters.

I lead Alex back there. A man is sitting in the booth, wearing a faded Crimson Tide hat pulled low. He's not doing much—just scrolling on a phone, a small plastic case sitting on the table next to a half-empty soda. He looks like any other guy in Blueville who peaked in high school and decided to make a living out of it. There's no edge to him, no menacing vibe. He's just a guy in a hat.

"This is Alex," I say. "She's in a spot."

The man looks up. He doesn't smile, but he isn't cold. He looks at Alex's slight frame, her messy hair, the way she's hovering just behind my shoulder.

"What kind of spot?" he asks. His voice is flat, conversational.

"Short on cash. Way short," I say. "But she's reliable. More reliable than anyone else you've got on the street."

The man looks at Alex again. "You know what the product is?"

Alex blinks. "Glitter?"

"The oculars," he says. He slides the plastic case an inch forward on the table. "It's a simple setup. I give you a starter supply. Three vials. You move them, you bring back the principal and twenty percent of the gross. You keep the rest. No quotas, no rush. Just a way to make a few hundred a week without having to clock in anywhere."

It sounds reasonable. It sounds like a favor.

Alex looks at me. I don't push her. I just lean back against the booth, keeping my expression open. I'm not telling her to do it; I'm just showing her the door is open.

"I can do that," Alex says. Her voice is steadier now.

The man nods and pushes the case toward her. "Keep them cool. Don't let the light hit the vials for too long. I'll see you next Tuesday."

As we walk out of the shop, the red awning is a blur in my peripheral vision. I can feel the tension leaving Alex's shoulders. She's holding the case in her hand, clutching it like a lifeline.

"You really think it'll be that easy?" she asks.

"Easier," I say.

The walk back to my place is slower. The sun is gone now, replaced by a deep, bruised purple sky. I stop for a second, reach into my pocket, and tilt my head back. I squeeze a single drop of Glitter into my left eye.

I blink.

Suddenly, the dark street isn't dark anymore. Tiny, brilliant white stars explode across my vision. Bright flashes of light flicker in the corners of my eyes, dancing in time with the beat of my heart. It's like a silent firework show happening behind my eyelids.

"What are you doing?" Alex asks.

"Just taking the edge off," I say. I turn to her, and I can see her watching me. She's looking at my eyes, probably seeing the way my pupils have blown wide. I can tell she wants to ask something, but she doesn't. She just smiles—a small, tentative thing.

My apartment is a one-bedroom that smells like old laundry and a vanilla candle that gave up hours ago. It's cluttered, with stacks of magazines and half-finished art projects taking up the floor space, but it's warm.

I find a t-shirt that's too big for her—an old grey thing from a concert years ago—and hand it over. Alex changes in the bathroom and comes back out, looking like a ghost in the oversized fabric.

She doesn't ask for a bed. She just sinks onto the floor, leaning her back against the side of the couch, using a pile of folded blankets as a makeshift pillow.

She lies back, staring up at the ceiling. The dog tag pendant rests flat on her chest, the silver metal catching the dim light of the room.

A few feet away, on the coffee table, the plastic case of Glitter sits perfectly still. Alex doesn't look at it. She just keeps staring at the ceiling, her breathing slow and deep, finally still.
