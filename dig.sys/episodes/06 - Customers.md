
# 6 — Customers

---

Alex is leaning over the counter, a chipped thrift-store mug in one hand. She is staring at the black Glitter case. I am in the chair, feet up, watching the dust motes dance in the yellow light coming through the blinds. The afternoon heat presses through the window. The air is still, smelling of old grease and the damp concrete of the building's exterior.

A knock. Not a loud one. Hesitant. Alex doesn't move for a second, then she sets the mug down and opens the door.

Steve is there. He is a wall of a man in a white t-shirt with a yellow stain under the arm. He takes up the whole doorframe, pushing the humidity
of the afternoon into the room with him. He doesn't look at her. He looks at the floor, then the peeling paint of the opposite wall.

"Hey," he says. His voice is a low rumble. "The other guy... he's not picking up. I thought maybe you had some."

Alex doesn't ask why the other guy is gone. She doesn't ask for a name. She just steps back and gestures to the counter. Steve follows, moving slow, his bulk making the small studio feel like a closet. He doesn't see me at first, and when he does, he gives a small nod. He doesn't say hello.

Alex reaches into the case. Three vials. She sets them on the counter in a
row. Then she holds out her hand.

"One hundred and twenty," she says.

I know the street rate. It is sixty for three. She is charging him double.
I watch Steve's face. He doesn't blink. He doesn't even look at the vials.
He just reaches into his pocket and pulls out the bills. He counts them out—crisp twenties—and lays them on the counter. He doesn't complain. He doesn't ask why the price changed.

Neither of them looks the other in the eye. They just stand there, the air heavy, until the money changes hands.

Steve scoops up the vials. He does not stay longer than the transaction requires. He turns and heads back out the door, his shoulder nearly brushing the frame.

The door clicks shut.

The apartment goes quiet. The jaundiced light stays on the counter, hitting the spot where the money was. Alex’s hand is still open, palm up.

---

The knock is light. Three taps.

Alex doesn't even look up from the table before she stands. She's got the vials laid out in a neat grid. Small glass cylinders of Glitter, caught in the yellow light of the living room. The cash is there too, a messy stack of twenties and tens. She broke the rule. She brought it home.

She opens the door. Two men.

The first one is mid-thirties, wearing a windbreaker that looks like it came from a thrift store. He has a face I've seen before. Not a specific memory, just a feeling. A guy at a gas station. A guy in the background of a grocery store. It's a blank-page face. Recognition without relationship.

The second man is bigger. A wall of a human in a grey hoodie. He doesn't say anything. He just steps inside and closes the door behind him.

"Hey," the first guy says. His voice is flat. "We heard you're the one to talk to about the drops."

Alex smiles. It's a small, naive thing. She starts to point back toward the table. "Yeah, I can help you with—"

The windbreaker guy doesn't let her finish. He doesn't yell. He doesn't even change his expression. He just reaches into his pocket and snaps open a serrated folding knife. The click is loud in the small room.

He steps forward and puts the edge against the side of Alex's neck.

I don't breathe. I can't. I'm frozen in the chair, my heart slamming against my ribs like a trapped bird. The big man in the hoodie moves. He doesn't hit me. He just grabs my shoulder and shoves me back into the seat, pinning me with the weight of his forearm across my chest. He's heavy. He smells like old cigarettes and damp concrete.

"The case," the guy with the knife says. "The cash. All of it. Now."

Alex is perfectly still. Her eyes are wide, staring at nothing. She doesn't scream. She doesn't fight. She just slowly raises her hands.

"The case is in the cabinet," she whispers.

The guy doesn't move the knife from her throat. "Get it."

She moves like a robot. Slow. Precise. She reaches into the cabinet and pulls out the main case of Glitter. She brings it back to the table and sets it down next to the cash.

The guy with the knife reaches over and scoops everything up. The vials, the money, the whole damn haul. He doesn't rush. He takes his time, making sure he has every last cent.

He pulls the knife away from Alex's skin. He looks at her for a second. He doesn't look angry. He doesn't even look mean. He just looks like a guy finishing a chore.

"Stay safe," he says.

He turns and walks out. The big man lets go of my shoulder and follows. The door closes. The lock clicks.

The silence that follows is heavy. It fills the room like water.

I break first. I slide off the chair and hit the floor, letting out a jagged, shaking breath. "Fucking... fucking hell," I hiss. I want to scream, but my throat is too dry.

Alex doesn't move. She's still standing by the table. She's staring at the empty space where the money was. Her hand is curled into a fist, clutching the dog tag pendant hanging from her neck. She's squeezing the metal so hard her knuckles are white.

The morning light is grey and filtered through the cracked blinds. The apartment smells like stale coffee and the kind of silence that happens after a disaster.

An overturned cushion. A knocked-over chair. The table is empty.

The crew is all here. Beth is fussing, moving around the small kitchen area with a bag of bagels and a stack of napkins. She's talking, but she's not saying anything that matters, just filling the air so it doesn't feel so dead. Diana is sitting on the edge of the sofa, a tablet in her lap, her fingers tapping rapidly across the glass as she scrolls through local police scanners or some dark-web forum.

Roni is pacing the length of the rug. She's in full-work mode. Concrete. "Exactly how many vials? Did they see the labels? Did they say where they were from?"

Sam is sitting quietly in the corner, leaning back against the wall. They aren't talking. They're just watching Alex.

Carla is here too. She's still in her clinic scrubs, a pale, exhausted blue. She looks like she hasn't slept in three days. She's leaning against the doorframe, her arms crossed, her expression plain. She's seen too many holes in people's lives to be surprised by this.

"I can't place him," I say, leaning back against the counter. I'm trying to piece it together. "He was just... a guy. Average. Forgettable. Mid-thirties. Windbreaker."

"Any marks?" Roni asks. "Tattoos? Scars?"

"Now that I think about it," I say, squinting. "His right wrist. He had this small, faded blue ink blotch. Like a star, or maybe just a smudge. Small. Old."

Carla doesn't move, but her eyes shift to Alex. "That's Ted."

The room goes quiet.

"You know him?" I ask.

"I've seen him selling near the clinic," Carla says. Her voice is flat, tired. "He's a bottom-feeder. Small time, but mean when he's desperate."

Alex looks up. Her light grey eyes are clouded. She speaks softly, almost to herself.

"I think I went to high school with him."

The words land quietly. No one moves. No one gasps. The silence thickens, a collective held breath that no one lets go.

"We should find him," Roni says, though she sounds less sure now.

"We can't," Diana says without looking up from the screen. "Not without a map. He's a ghost in the system."

"We have to tell Jaw-man," Beth says, her voice trembling slightly. "If he knows the product is gone..."

Alex nods. She doesn't look at any of us. She just looks at the empty table.

The light through the blinds casts thin, striped shadows across the wood. The dog tag pendant rests against her chest, a small piece of silver catching the morning sun.
