
# 9c — Wade (Collapse)
**POV: Becky**

---

Beth is mid-sentence, her voice hitting that honey-thick pitch that makes the pilgrims lean in. She's gesturing toward a crumbling brick facade, weaving a story about the President as a teenager–some lie about a secret meeting in an alleyway–and the family is eating it up. The father in the khaki vest is nodding. The mother has the map folded tight. The kids are squinting against the glare, captivated.

Wade doesn't wait for a pause. He doesn't wait for a transition. He just walks into the center of the circle, cutting through the space between Beth and the father. He doesn't look at her.

"You actually paying for this?" Wade asks. He's looking straight at the father.

The flow of the story snaps. Beth stops, her hand still frozen in the air.

"Excuse me?" the father says. He looks at Wade, then back to Beth.

Wade shifts his weight. The faded Blueville High varsity jacket is bleached pale by the sun, the letters cracking across his chest. He looks at the father with a flat, bored expression.

"Beth Miller," Wade says. He says the full name. "The Millers didn't move into the county until 2036. They came up from those Mobile trailer parks."

The silence that follows is immediate. It's a hard, sudden vacuum. 

"I was born and raised–" Beth starts, but the voice is wrong. It's too high.

"They lived three towns over," Wade says, his voice plain. "Not on the next street. Not for three generations. Not even close."

The father's face changes. He doesn't shout. He doesn't even look angry at 
first. He just looks tired. He looks at the dilapidated storefronts and then at Beth.

The mother lowers the map. She doesn't say anything. She just reaches out and takes the kids' hands. The children start to ask something, and she shushes them, a sharp, quiet sound that cuts through the heat.

The father looks at Beth.

"I'd like my money back, please," he says.

The smile doesn't slide off Beth's face; it just stops. The muscles in her 
cheeks go slack. Her shoulders drop an inch. She doesn't try to recover. She doesn't try to pivot the story or offer a discount to keep them on the 
tour. She just goes still.

She reaches into her back pocket. She pulls out four twenties. She holds them out to the father.

"Eighty dollars," she says. She looks at the pavement. "I'm sorry."

The father takes the money without a word. He doesn't thank her. He doesn't complain. He just turns and walks away. The mother follows, pulling the kids with her, moving fast toward the main road. They disappear around the corner in a matter of seconds.

The silence that follows is physical. The space where the family was standing feels empty, like a hole had been punched in the afternoon.

Wade doesn't reach for the money. He doesn't ask for a cut. He just stands 
there, watching Beth.

"Don't run any more pilgrims here," Wade says. "I'll see you at the next site."

He turns and walks off. The last thing I see is the back of that varsity jacket as he rounds the corner, disappearing into the glare.

Roni steps out from the shade of the lamp post twenty yards away. She doesn't ask what happened. She doesn't ask where the family went. She just 
falls into step beside us.

We start the walk back.

No one speaks. There is no banter. There is no plan to make the money up. There is just the sound of our shoes on the cracked pavement and the distant hum of traffic.

Beth's hand stays in her back pocket. Her fingers find nothing but lint and denim. She doesn't take her hand out.

I watch Alex. She's walking a step behind Beth, her gaze fixed on the back 
of Beth's head. Her fingers are twitching. Her hand drifts up, gripping the dog tag pendant at her throat, squeezing the metal.

"We can try the harbor," Alex says.

Her voice is small. It doesn't sound like a suggestion. It sounds like she's trying to remember how to speak.

Beth doesn't answer. 

We reach the edge of the neighborhood. The late afternoon light is long and yellow, casting deep shadows across the road. Beth is still staring straight ahead, her hand buried in that empty pocket.
