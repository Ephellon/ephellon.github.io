# 8 – Hometown
**POV: Becky**

---

Seven people in a room built for two is a recipe for sweat. We are all crowded into Alex's living room, but the center of the space remains an island. The table is still there, but it is an absence. It is the spot where Ted took the stock and the cash, and now we all just step around it, as if the air there is still contaminated.

Alex stands by the window, looking out at the street. She doesn't look at us. She says it once, her voice flat.

"Ten thousand dollars. Thirty days."

The silence that follows is heavy. Roni leans against the wall, crossing her arms. Diana is perched on the edge of a chair, her tablet open on her lap. Beth is sitting on the floor, chin in her hand, while Sam and Carla hover near the door.

"And then what?" Sam asks. They keep their voice low. "What happens on day thirty-one?"

Alex doesn't answer. She doesn't move. She just keeps looking at the street. The lack of a response is the only answer we get.

"I can hit a few stores," Roni says. "Some quick grabs. Jewelry, small electronics. I know three spots on the edge of town with blind spots in the security."

"Too slow," I say. "Ten thousand isn't a few grabs. It's a heist. Too much heat for that kind of money."

Carla speaks up from the door. "The shipping warehouses over by the docks are hiring. Temp labor. Heavy lifting, long hours, but it's steady pay."

"Wrong size," I say, glancing at Alex and then myself. "Look at us. The warehouses want bulk. They want guys who can move crates without snapping in half. We'd be the first ones fired or the first ones crushed."

Diana taps her screen, her eyes flickering up. "Remote work. Low risk. Alex, you could do the online thing. Content, subscriptions. It's fast money if you can play the angle."

I look at Alex. I look at the way she is holding her shoulders, the way she stands.

"You're not the look," I say.

Alex doesn't argue. She doesn't even blink.

"The pilgrims," Beth says suddenly. She is still on the floor, looking up at the group. "Look at the trickle of them every day. They come in from all over, looking for any scrap of the President's ghost. They have money, and they're bored, and they want to feel like they've seen something 'authentic.'"

Beth stands up. "The tour-agent scheme. We don't just show them the markers. We sell them the 'Real Blueville' experience. The hidden spots. The stories the brochures leave out because they're too messy for the history books."

Diana looks at her tablet. "I can handle the parts that need to look legit. A landing page, some fake testimonials, maybe a digital map that looks official. I can make us look like a boutique agency."

"I can be the scout," Roni says. "I'll walk the route, spot the cops, and keep an eye on the crowds. I'll tell you when the coast is clear to approach."

"So Beth fronts," I say, taking stock. "Diana on tech. Roni scouts. Alex, you tag along. You're the 'local apprentice' or whatever. You need to see how this works."

Alex finally turns away from the window. She nods once.

"And I'm always tagging along," I add.

"I can't," Carla says. "I've got the double shift at the clinic. People aren't getting any healthier."

"I'll stay too," Sam says. "Someone needs to hold down the home base in case Jaw-man decides to drop by for a progress report."

The plan is set. We move out, leaving the empty table behind. We walk through the afternoon air toward the center of town, the heat pressing into our skin.

---

The President's childhood home is a small white cottage with a white picket fence that looks too clean to be real. There is a historical marker in the grass, a slab of bronze and stone that tells a sterilized version of a life. A few families are huddled around it, clutching colorful brochures and squinting against the bright Alabama sun. They wear crisp polos and walking shoes, clutching digital cameras.

Roni is twenty yards ahead, leaning against a lamp post. She gives a small, nearly invisible nod.

Beth steps forward. The slouch vanishes, replaced by a wide, beaming smile and a tilt of the head. She approaches a family of four—a father in a khaki vest, a mother holding a map, and two children who look like they are melting.

"Excuse me! Hi there," Beth says, her voice honey-thick. "I couldn't help but notice you're looking at the marker. I'm Beth. I was born and raised right here in Blueville—three generations of my family lived on the very next street over."

The father looks up, blinking.

"We're just trying to see the main sites," the father says.

"The main sites are the same ones everyone sees," Beth says, leaning in. "If you want the real local tour—the places that aren't in the brochures, the spots where the President actually spent his time before he was a statue—I can show you. It's a walking tour, about an hour. I'll show you the side of this town the history books forgot."

The father looks at his kids, then back at Beth. "How much?"

"Twenty dollars per person," Beth says. "Cash, on the spot. For a local's perspective."

The father reaches into his wallet and pulls out four twenty-dollar bills. He hands them to Beth. The mother sighs and folds her map, but the children are already bouncing on their heels.

"Follow me," Beth says.

As they start walking, Beth begins the narration. She points toward a cluster of old oaks near a dry creek bed.

"Now, the brochures will tell you he spent his afternoons studying the classics," Beth says, her voice projecting. "But the truth is, he used to sneak out to this creek. He'd hide behind those oaks, smoking stolen cigarettes and reading banned poetry. He didn't want to be the golden boy; he wanted to be the ghost of Blueville."

The father is nodding, absorbing every word.

I walk a few paces behind, watching. I am not looking at the family or the house. I am looking at Alex.

Alex is walking in silence. She is looking at the father's hand, specifically the way he had held the cash before handing it over. Her eyes are fixed on that spot. Her fingers twitch against the side of her leg, a rhythmic, nervous motion.

They turn a corner, moving away from the marker and toward a row of dilapidated storefronts.

I glance across the street. A man is leaning against a rusted fence, wearing a faded Blueville High varsity jacket from a decade ago. He isn't a tourist. He is a local. He is watching them—not the family, but Beth and Alex. He leans back, head tilted, tracking their movement with a slow, steady focus.

I look back at Alex. She has noticed him too. She doesn't look away, but she doesn't speak. She just keeps walking, the twitch in her hand continuing.
