
# 11c – Lift
**POV: Becky**

---

The tablet on the coffee table shows the date. Day 5.

The apartment is warm. The mid-afternoon light comes through the cracked blinds in sharp, slanted bars, cutting across the floor. It is different from the morning light—harder, more yellow. The table has been cleared. The bagels Carla brought are gone. The only thing remaining on the laminate counter is the black Glitter case.

Roni is standing. She has the legal pad from yesterday. She doesn't look at the case; she looks at the rest of us. Her voice is in that audit tone, 
the one she uses when she’s counting inventory or explaining a mistake.

"We aren't running product yet," Roni says. "That stays on the table. We start smaller. A lift."

Diana leans back in the chair, her eyes on Roni. "A lift. Where?"

"The overlook," Roni says. "The presidential spot. Mid-afternoon, heat-haze on the road. Pilgrims park their SUVs, walk to the railing, take 
a picture of the plaque, and walk back. They leave their bags in the seats. They leave wallets in the open."

Sam shifts their weight. "We've never actually stolen anything. Not like this."

"It’s a smaller step," Roni says. "Less risk than the product run, but it puts cash in the pot. It’s better than sitting here waiting for the house to win."

Diana doesn't smile. Roni doesn't either. 

Beth is standing near the door. She isn't looking at Roni. She is looking at the floor, her weight shifted to one heel, her hands gripping the strap 
of her bag. She has been vibrating since Day 4, the kind of stillness that 
isn't actually still.

"I can't," Beth says.

She just says it, and then she turns. She walks out of the apartment. The door closes behind her with a soft click.

Roni doesn't acknowledge the exit. She doesn't even look at the door. A fly buzzes against the screen 
of the window, a frantic, repetitive sound. 

Roni looks at the legal pad one last time, tapping the pen against the spiral. The refrigerator hums, a low, rattling sound that seems to fill the room now that the conversation has stopped. The yellow light on the floor doesn't move.

"Sam, you're the hands," Roni says. "Diana, you're the eyes. I'll coordinate. We leave now."

They move out in a synchronized blur. The door closes again.

It is just Alex and me.

The apartment is quiet. The hum of the refrigerator fills the gap where the voices used to be. Alex is by the window, standing in one of the bars of light. She doesn't look at me. She is looking at the counter.

Time passes. I watch the light move. The yellow bars shift across the linoleum, sliding toward the wall. Outside, a car horn blares twice on the 
main road, a distant, tinny noise. The air in the apartment is stagnant, smelling of old coffee and the faint, metallic scent of the radiator. Alex 
doesn't move for a long time. She stands there, her hands hanging at her sides, her gaze fixed on the matte black lid of the case.

Then, her foot shifts. The sole of her shoe makes a small, sticky sound against the linoleum. She takes one step, then another. She stops two feet 
from the counter, her hand hovering in the air for a second before she reaches for the plastic.

She walks to the counter. Her movements are slow. She reaches out and flips the latch. The sound is loud in the silence. She lifts the lid.

Inside, the vials sit in the foam. They are small, clear glass, filled with the shimmering liquid. She extends her index finger and presses the tip of it against the side of the center vial. 

She holds it there. Her finger stays against the glass for three seconds. Four. 

She doesn't breathe. I watch her chest, held still.

She pulls her finger away. She closes the lid and presses the latch down until it clicks. She just turns and walks back to her spot by the window.

I don't say anything. I don't ask her what she's doing. I just watch her stand there again, the light now hitting the side of her face, her eyes returning to the case.

An hour later, the door opens.

The door swings open and the mid-afternoon heat pushes into the room, a thick wave of salt and exhaust. Sam pushes the door shut, the wood still warm to the touch from the sun. Their boots make a heavy, rhythmic sound on the linoleum, a contrast to the silence of the last hour.

Roni, Diana, and Sam come back in. They bring in the smell of hot asphalt and salt-sweat. Sam drops onto the sofa, the cushions hissing as they take the weight. Diana stays leaning against the wall, her chest moving in slow, deep rhythms. Roni stands in the center of the room, still holding the legal pad.

"The take," Roni says, her voice returning to that operational clip. "One hundred and twelve dollars. Mostly twenties and tens. A Visa card—useless. 
Three receipts from a gas station in Mobile. And this."

Roni holds up a driver's license. A man in his fifties, smiling in a way that looks like he peaked in a high school yearbook. Roni pockets the ID without comment.

"How was it?" I ask.

"Too close," Diana says. She is leaning against the wall, her face flushed 
from the heat. "We had the bag open, Sam was reaching in, and this SUV parked right next to us. A dog started barking from inside the car—just screaming. Sam almost jumped out of their skin. I thought the driver was going to open the door right on top of them."

Sam nods. "It was loud. I think I dropped the wallet once."

"But you got it," Roni says. She sets the cash on the table, the bills slightly crumpled.

The crew settles into the apartment. Sam goes to the kitchen for water, their movements jerky. Diana starts scrolling on her tablet, the screen casting a blue light against the yellow of the room. Roni looks at the cash, then at the legal pad, then at Alex. 

Alex hasn't moved from the window. She isn't looking at the money. 

The light has shifted again, casting a long shadow from the counter across 
the laminate. The black case sits there, closed, its surface absorbing the 
remaining afternoon sun. 

Alex keeps her eyes on the plastic.
