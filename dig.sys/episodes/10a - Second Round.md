# 10a - Second Round
**POV: Becky**

---

Roni looks at her watch. "Ten minutes until they hit the marker," she says. Diana taps a tablet screen. "Wade's tip matches the transit logs for the luxury vans. It's a go." Beth stands in the center of the room, reciting a line about local heritage. Her voice dips into a slow, honeyed drawl.

Roni walks with her shoulders dropped, her gaze shifting from the road to the tree line. Beth's stride is wide. They move in a line, keeping a consistent distance between one another. Their pace remains constant.

Wade leans against the fence post, the faded varsity jacket shifting as he moves his weight. He nods. Beth nods back. No one speaks.

A family stands by the historical marker. The father wears a crisp linen shirt and leather loafers. The mother has a wide-brimmed straw hat and gold jewelry at her wrists. A grandmother stands behind them in oversized dark sunglasses. Two teenagers lean against a rental SUV, wearing brand-name sneakers that have not yet touched dirt. The father holds a DSLR camera.

Beth steps forward. She does not pause.

"The brochure says this was a place of humble beginnings," Beth says, gesturing toward the scrub. "But the humble part was a choice. If you want the real Blueville—the stories the brochures scrub out—that's a different walk."

The father lowers the camera. He looks at the mother.

"What does that involve?" the mother asks.

"A walk through the actual geography of the place," Beth says. "The parts that explain why the President is the way he is. Forty dollars per person."

The father looks at the grandmother, then back to Beth. He reaches for a leather wallet.

Diana steps onto the grass. She looks at the marker, then at the screen of her tablet. She nods to Beth. She turns and walks back toward the street.

"Follow me," Beth says.

She leads them toward a dip in the terrain. The family follows, their footsteps heavy on the dry earth.

"Look at the slope of that ridge," Beth says, pointing to a patch of dead grass. "That's where the old drainage pipes burst in '92. The town just let it sink. That's how you learn where the power actually sits in this county. The water went where the money was."

The father leans in. He takes a photo of the dip in the land. The teenagers stop looking at their phones and watch the scrub.

"And here," Beth says, stopping by a line of old fencing. "The brochure calls this a 'rustic boundary.' Look at the wire. It's rusted, but the posts are cedar. Only the people who knew the mayor in the nineties got cedar. Everyone else got pine that rotted in five years. You can tell who was in the inner circle just by looking at the wood."

The grandmother pushes her sunglasses up. She nods.

Beth leads them back to the marker. "That's the condensed version. The full history is usually a bit more depressing, but this is the gist of it."

The father reaches into his wallet. He hands Beth a stack of bills.

Two hundred dollars in total.

Beth reaches into the pile and pulls out forty dollars. She holds it out. Wade takes it without ceremony, sliding the bills into the pocket of the varsity jacket. He doesn't speak. He just watches them leave.

The crew walks back toward the apartment. Roni keeps her gaze on the road. Diana is already ahead of them, her pace quick.

Beth stops and counts the money. One hundred and sixty dollars remains.

Alex looks at the bills. She smiles. The expression stays on her face. Her hand rests near the dog tag pendant, fingers loose. She does not look away from the money.

The sun stays high. Alex keeps walking.
