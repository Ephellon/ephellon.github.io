
# 7 — Ten Thousand
**POV: Becky**

---

The sun is a white hammer hitting the cinderblock. Heat shimmers off the pavement in long, distorted waves, blurring the edges of the Wash-N-Go. It
is a clear day, the kind of Gulf Coast heat that feels like it is trying to bleach the color out of everything. The lot is empty except for us and a single, distant attendant leaning against a vacuum station, eyes hidden behind dark lenses, as far away from us as the property allows.

Alex sent the message through the Smoke Shop three hours ago. She didn't tell me what she said, only that he agreed to meet. She has been standing in the same spot for twenty minutes, her slight frame held rigid, her short brown hair sticking to the back of her neck.

The black Silverado rolls into the lot. It doesn't speed. It moves with a slow, heavy inevitability. When the engine cuts, the silence that follows is immediate and absolute.

Jacob steps out of the cab. He is wearing a polo shirt that fits him perfectly, the fabric crisp despite the heat. On his wrist, the matte black watch looks like a piece of industrial equipment. He doesn't look at
us at first. He just stands there, hands scarred at the knuckles, shoulders slumped in a way that suggests he is carrying a weight that doesn't belong to him.

As he turns toward Alex, the light hits his face. His lower jaw projects forward, an offset ledge of bone that pushes the rest of his features out of alignment. He doesn't look like a predator today. He looks tired.

Alex doesn't wait for him to speak. She doesn't move, but her voice is steady, devoid of the tremor I expected.

"It was Ted," she says. "He came to the apartment. He took everything. All
the stock. All the cash."

Jacob doesn't interrupt. He doesn't even blink.

"I know who he is," Alex continues. "I think I went to high school with him. He has a small blue ink blotch, a tattoo, on his right wrist. He took
it all, Jacob. I don't have anything left."

She isn't lying. She isn't bargaining. She is just handing him the facts, stripped bare.

Jacob doesn't react. He doesn't shout, and he doesn't threaten. He simply turns his head and looks past Alex, staring at the shimmering horizon where the heat haze meets the flat line of the town. He stays like that for a long beat. The only sound is the distant, rhythmic thrum of a vacuum
cleaner somewhere at the edge of the lot.

He looks back at her. His voice is flat, devoid of theatricality.

"I have a business to run," he says. "And there are people I answer to. They don't care how it happened. They care that it happened."

Alex's hand moves. Her fingers find the dog tag pendant hanging around her
neck, clutching the metal tight against her skin. She doesn't look away from him.

"Ten thousand dollars," Jacob says. "Thirty days."

He doesn't explain what happens on day thirty-one. He doesn't have to. The
vacuum of the unspoken is heavier than any threat he could have voiced. The number hangs in the air between them, a cold, mathematical fact.

Jacob looks at Alex. He holds the gaze a second longer than necessary. There is no warmth there, just a recognition of a pattern. He exhales, a long, slow breath that seems to deflate his entire posture. He looks like he wants to say something–something that isn't a directive–but the moment passes. He doesn't say it.

"Right," he says.

He turns, steps back into the Silverado, and closes the door. The engine starts with a low growl, and the truck pulls away slowly, leaving a trail of heat and exhaust in the shimmering air.

Alex is still holding the dog tag. She doesn't move until the truck is gone.

I look at her, and then I look at the sun. The light hasn't changed, but the air feels different. The space between where we are and where we need to be has suddenly shrunk. There are fewer days in the world than there were ten minutes ago.

Alex lets go of the pendant. It swings once, catching the light, and then settles against her chest.
