
# 9b — Wade (Friction)
**POV: Becky**

---

Beth is in the middle of a story about the President's first car–a rusted-out Chevy that he supposedly stripped from a junkyard and rebuilt in a shed–and the family is eating it up. The father is nodding, his khaki
vest damp with sweat, while the two kids lean in, their faces sticky with melting popsicles. Roni is twenty yards ahead, a silhouette against the glare, leaning on a lamp post and scanning the street.

The sun hits the cracked pavement of the center district, baking the smell
of old asphalt and saltwater into the air. We're passing a row of storefronts with boards over the windows and "For Lease" signs from the nineties, heading toward the dry creek bed. Beth's voice is honey-thick, projecting just enough to keep the family focused on her and not the peeling paint.

Wade steps out from the shadow of a closed pharmacy. He doesn't run or shout. He just drifts into the path, moving with a slow, hometown confidence. He's wearing that varsity jacket, the wool faded to a dull navy, the letters on the chest cracked and peeling. In this heat, the jacket is a commitment to a version of himself that died a decade ago.

"Beth Holloway," Wade says.

The name hits the air and stays there. Beth stops mid-sentence. She doesn't jump, but her shoulders lock. The family stops too. The father's expression shifts; the eager, touristy smile vanishes, replaced by a sudden, sharp curiosity. The mother lowers the map she's been clutching, her eyes darting between Beth and the man in the jacket.

"Wade," Beth says. Her voice is a fraction higher than it was a second ago.

Wade smiles, but it doesn't reach his eyes. He keeps his hands in his pockets. "I thought I recognized that voice. Saw your mama at the Piggly Wiggly last Tuesday. She looks good. Told her you were doing something with the city, but I didn't realize it was tours."

The father doesn't say anything, but he takes a half-step back. He looks at Beth–really looks at her–and the read is clear. The "three generations on the next street over" pitch just lost its shine. The kids don't notice;
they're too busy trying to lick the red syrup off their wrists, but the adults have gone cold.

Beth's smile returns, though it doesn't quite reach the corners of her eyes. She lets out a short, airy laugh and gestures toward Wade.

"Wade here is my unofficial regional historian," she says, her tone sliding back into the guide-persona, though it sounds thinner now. "He handles the deep archives–the stuff they don't want in the brochures. He's
just checking to make sure I'm not omitting any of the *really* scandalous
bits."

Wade lets out a dry huff. He doesn't laugh, but he doesn't push. He's made
his mark.

"Anyway," Wade says, stepping back into the shade. "I'll let you finish your tour. Don't want to steal your thunder."

He doesn't leave. He just moves a few feet away, leaning against a brick wall and watching.

Beth pivots back to the family. "Now, if we follow this alley, we hit the Founder's Bridge. That's where the real history happens."

She keeps talking, but she's working harder now. Her gestures are bigger, her anecdotes more frantic. The father isn't taking photos anymore. He's not asking questions. The mother is staring at the map again, though she isn't really looking at the lines.

We reach the bridge. It's a concrete slab over a creek bed that hasn't seen water in three years, filled with grey silt and discarded tires. Beth
delivers her closer–a poignant little summary about the resilience of Blueville and the spirit of the Gulf–and she holds out her hand for the final farewell.

The father shakes her hand briefly. The grip is short. There's no comment about the "real Blueville" or the authenticity of the experience.

"We have what we need," the mother says. "Thank you."

They turn and walk back toward the main road. The kids are still chattering, oblivious, but the parents are quiet. They don't ask about other tours. They don't ask for a business card. They just leave.

The eighty dollars they paid upfront is already in Beth's back pocket.

Wade walks back over. He has the same casual pace, his sleeves pushed up against the heat, looking like he has nowhere else to be. He stops a few feet from us.

"Listen," Wade says. His voice is plain, devoid of the performance Beth uses. "Let me know next time you're working a family. Maybe we can split something. Or maybe I find out and we don't."

He isn't asking for a cut of today's money. He isn't threatening to hit her. But the implication sits there, heavy and static. He's claiming the sidewalk.

"I'll keep it in mind, Wade," Beth says.

Wade nods once and walks off the way he came, his varsity jacket disappearing around the corner of the pharmacy. He doesn't look back.

Roni drifts back to us from her post, her expression blank. "What happened?"

"Nothing," Beth says. "Just a local."

Roni absorbs that. She doesn't ask for details. She just looks at the empty street where the family disappeared.

Beth reaches into her pocket and pulls out the cash. She lays the bills out on the concrete ledge of the bridge. Eighty dollars gross. Eighty dollars net.

She counts them once. Then she counts them again. She pauses, her thumb lingering on the edge of a ten-dollar bill, before she slowly folds the money and slides it back into her pocket. She's quieter than usual. The usual post-gig energy–the immediate planning for the next mark, the adrenaline of the hustle–isn't there.

I look at Alex. She's watching the money, then watching Beth, then watching me. She looks small against the backdrop of the dead creek.

"Did we do it right?" Alex asks. Her voice is soft, barely a whisper.

Beth doesn't answer immediately. She just stares at the dry creek bed.

We start the walk back. There's less talking than there was on the way there. Beth isn't narrating anything. I watch her gaze drift toward the horizon, her eyes empty for a second before she blinks and snaps back.

Alex's hand is gripped tight around the dog tag pendant at her neck. Beth has the eighty dollars folded once in her grip, clutching the paper tight.
