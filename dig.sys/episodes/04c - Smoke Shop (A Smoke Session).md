# 4c – Smoke Shop (A Smoke Session)
**POV: Becky**

---

The humidity in Blueville doesn't just sit; it clings. It's a wet, hot wool blanket draped over everything. I walk Alex down the cracked sidewalk, the soles of my sandals slapping the concrete. She's quiet, her shoulders hunched, looking like a bird that just got tossed out of its nest. She's still wearing the same oversized band shirt from three days ago.

I've done this a dozen times with a dozen different people. Find someone who's hit a wall, take them somewhere the music is too loud to think, and let them drift for a few hours. It's not a plan. It's just how we survive the summer.

The session is at T-Bone's place. T-Bone played center for the Blueville High Tigers in 2032. Now he owns a house with a sagging porch and a small army of people who still call him "Big Bone." The porch is crowded. Low-slung lawn chairs and old milk crates serve as seating. A small table in the center holds a few lukewarm beers and a small, clear bottle of Glitter.

The music is some generic chart-topper, a synth-pop beat that Alex clearly hates. She doesn't say it, but she has that slight pinch in her brow. I lead her to a corner, leaning against the railing.

Around us, the regulars are in various stages of fade. There's Marcus, still wearing a varsity jacket that's three sizes too small and ten years out of date. There's Sarah, who was the head cheerleader until she wasn't. They're all just ghosts of their senior year. A few of them have the Glitter in their eyes—tiny, shimmering stars dancing in their pupils, making them look at things that aren't there.

The dealer is a guy with a single gold tooth and a clean polo shirt. He doesn't push. He doesn't hustle. He just hangs out, leaning against the siding of the house, moving with the casual efficiency of a bartender. He hands over a small vial, takes a folded twenty, and gives a quick, professional nod. No one is shouting. No one is panicked. It's just business.

I look at Alex. She isn't drifting.

She's staring. Not at the people, but at the gold-tooth guy. Her eyes follow every hand-off. She watches the way the money changes hands. She tracks the flow of people coming and going from the porch.

"You okay?" I ask.

Alex doesn't look at me. She's still tracking the dealer. "How much does he make in a night?" she asks.

I blink. "I don't know. Enough to keep the polo clean, I guess."

Alex doesn't respond. She just keeps watching. She isn't nodding along to the music.

I expect her to just sink into the couch and let the night swallow her, the way she usually does when things get bad. But Alex stands up. She doesn't ask me for an introduction. She doesn't check if I'm coming. She just walks straight over to the gold-tooth guy.

I stay where I am, watching from the railing.

Alex stops a few feet from him. She's small, slight, looking almost fragile next to him. She says something. I can't hear the words, but I see the dealer's reaction. He stops mid-transaction, looks her up and down, and then a small, amused smile creases his face. He doesn't look threatened. He looks like he just saw a kitten try to fight a dog.

He says something back. Alex doesn't flinch. She doesn't look away. She just stands her ground, her chin tilted up.

The dealer doesn't say yes. He shakes his head slightly and gestures with a thumb toward the street. He tells her he can't do it here, but he'll set something up.

Forty minutes later, we're standing under a red awning.

The Smoke Shop is a relic. The yellow signage is faded to the color of an old bruise, and a blue neon "OPEN" sign flickers in the window, casting a rhythmic, cold light over the sidewalk.

Inside, the air smells like stale tobacco and cheap incense. Q is behind the counter. He's an old man who looks like he's been carved out of driftwood. He doesn't look up when we walk in. He doesn't acknowledge Alex. He just continues wiping a glass surface with a rag, his movements slow and deliberate. He knows everything that happens in this zip code, and he doesn't say a word of it.

The gold-tooth guy is already there. He's leaning on the counter.

"She's got nerve," the dealer says. He doesn't say it as a compliment; he says it as an observation.

He reaches under the counter and pulls out a small, sealed starter supply. He lays it on the glass. He explains the terms—the commission, the drop-offs, the rules about not skimming. There's no menace in his voice. No threats of broken legs. He's just pitching a business model to a new employee.

Alex listens. She doesn't interrupt. When he finishes, she reaches out and takes the supply.

The walk back to my apartment is different. The humidity is still there, but Alex isn't fighting it. She's bouncing on her feet. There's a lightness in her step I haven't seen since the eviction. She's talking about "scaling" and "client lists," her voice energized and fast. She looks like she just won a lottery she entered without a ticket.

I walk beside her, silent. She usually follows my lead. Usually, she's the one asking where we're going. This time, she's the one with the map.

Later, we're back at my place. The AC is humming a low, dying rattle. Alex is sprawled out on the floor, her back against the side of my couch.

She has her device out—the screen is a spiderweb of cracks—and she's scrolling furiously. She's making lists. I can see the flickering light of the screen reflecting in her grey eyes.

She's smiling. It's a real smile, the kind that reaches her eyes and makes her look young and dangerous.

Her dog tag pendant has slid forward, resting against the center of her chest, catching the dim light of the room.

For a second, she stops. She sets the device down on the carpet and stares at the ceiling. The smile fades. Her face goes flat, blank, a momentary lapse in the energy.

Then she picks the device back up. The smile comes back, wider this time. She starts typing again.
