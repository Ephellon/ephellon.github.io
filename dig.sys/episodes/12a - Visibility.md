
# 12a — Visibility
<strong class="meta-line">POV  &bull;  Becky</strong>

---

The heat presses into the skin before we even hit the curb. Cicadas are already screaming in the oaks across the street, a high, thin vibration that fills the gaps between breaths. We walk toward the clapboard church, the same route as last time. The dying elm is still the only place with a shadow, its limbs grey and skeletal against the white sky.

Wade is there, leaning against the white fence in the faded varsity jacket. He doesn't move as we approach. He doesn't offer a greeting. He just looks at us, then looks past us to the road.

"Four hundred," Wade says. His voice is a flat list. "The quiet one. She speaks. Minimal."

I look at Alex. She doesn't change. She doesn't ask who "the quiet one" is, and she doesn't ask why she has to speak. She just stands there, holding that same stillness she brought with her from the apartment. We don't negotiate. We just wait.

The pilgrim is a woman. She wears a tailored cream silk wrap and gold sandals that make no sound on the gravel. She has a heavy watch on her wrist and moves with a slow, deliberate precision. She doesn't look at Wade. She doesn't look at me. Her eyes find Alex immediately. She holds the gaze for a second too long, then nods.

Beth steps forward. The Sunday-school-teacher voice is back, smooth and devoid of edge.

"The discipline of the threshold," Beth says, gesturing toward the church door. "The act of stepping over. The preparation of the mind before the entry."

We follow them inside. The wavy window glass distorts the trees outside, smearing the greens and greys of the oaks across the pane. The air inside smells of old wax and damp wood. 

The pilgrim moves slowly, her hand hovering inches from the surfaces. She stops at a heavy, iron-latched door in the hallway. She doesn't open it; she just touches the metal.

The pilgrim looks at Alex.

"Cold," Alex says.

The word is small. It fits exactly into the silence. The pilgrim doesn't ask for more. She doesn't smile. She just accepts the word and moves on.

Beth continues the script. "The stillness of the inner chamber. The refusal of the noise. The capacity to exist without the need for one's own 
voice."

They move into the small side room with the peeling wallpaper. The pilgrim 
reaches out and brushes the edge of a floral pattern where the paper has curled away from the plaster. She looks at Alex again.

"Peeling," Alex says.

Beth glances at Alex. A brief, observable check. She does not break the cadence. She does not improvise.

The tour ends by the heavy oak table near the back. The pilgrim reaches into a small bag and produces four hundred dollars. The bills are crisp, laid out on the wood without ceremony. No counting. No hesitation.

Wade steps in. He slides one hundred dollars into his jacket pocket. The other three hundred go to the crew. He doesn't explain the cut. He doesn't 
describe the people above him. He just takes his share and steps back toward the fence.

The walk back is a long stretch of shimmering asphalt. Alex's stride is rhythmic. Mechanical. One foot, then the other, her gaze fixed on a point somewhere above the horizon. She doesn't talk about the woman. She doesn't 
talk about the money. She just keeps that pace, her arms swinging in a narrow, controlled arc.

Back at the apartment, the air is stagnant. Alex goes straight to the window. 

The Glitter case is on the counter where it always sits. Closed. 

Alex stands there, looking at the case. Her hand moves up, fingers brushing the dog tag pendant at her neck. She doesn't move for a long time. She just watches the case.
