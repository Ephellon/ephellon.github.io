# 5 – Down Payment
**POV: Becky**

---

The Wash-N-Go is a slab of cracked pavement and grey gravel. Heat shimmers off the cinderblock walls, blurring the edges of the rusted chain-link fence. It's an overcast afternoon, the kind of Gulf Coast heavy where the air feels like a wet towel pressed against the skin. No rain yet, just the humidity.

Alex stands beside me, shifting her weight. She's wearing an oversized t-shirt and those beat-up boots. She keeps touching the dog tag pendant at her throat. I watch a man across the lot scrubbing a sedan with a rhythmic, mindless motion. The attendant leans against a vacuum station, chewing on a toothpick, eyes half-closed.

Then the Silverado pulls in.

It's black. Too black for this lot. The paint is a mirror, reflecting the dead grass and the grey sky. The engine cuts, and the silence that follows is sudden and heavy.

Jacob gets out.

He doesn't rush. He closes the door with a soft, metallic click. He's wearing a dark polo that fits him tight across the shoulders and a matte black watch that looks like it costs more than my car. His knuckles are scarred, a map of old collisions across the skin.

Then I see the jaw. The bone on the left side is pushed out, offset and heavy. It doesn't align with the rest of his face. When he looks at us, the displacement makes his expression read as a permanent, cold skepticism. He doesn't look at me. I'm just a shadow standing next to the product.

He stops three feet from Alex.

"You're the one," he says.

Alex nods. She doesn't speak.

He reaches into the truck and produces a thick roll of bills bound by a yellow rubber band. He holds it out. Alex takes it. Her hand shakes, just a tremor, but she grips the cash tight. Then he hands her a small, flat black case.

The Glitter.

"North Shore, the Flats, and the corridor between the old mill and the highway," Jacob says. His voice is a low, measured drone. "That's your territory. You check in Tuesday and Friday. If the count is short, we don't do phone calls. I come find you."

Alex swallows. "I understand."

Jacob doesn't move for a second. He looks at her. He holds the gaze a beat too long, his eyes scanning her short, messy hair and the slight frame of her shoulders. He opens his mouth, his jaw shifting, and for a moment it looks like he might actually say something — something that isn't a directive. Then he stops. He closes his mouth. He's seen a hundred girls like her start this walk.

He turns without another word and gets back in the truck. The Silverado pulls away, leaving a swirl of dust and the smell of expensive exhaust.

The lot is quiet again.

"Well," I say, nudging her arm. "Welcome to the corporate world. Let's get you moved in before the humidity melts us into the asphalt."

The new place is on the Smoke Shop end of town. It's a studio with walls that were white once, now a jaundiced, peeling yellow. The carpet is a stained tan, smelling of old cigarettes and dampness. There's one window with cracked plastic blinds that let in slivers of grey light.

Alex drops her nylon backpack in the center of the room. It's all she has.

"I brought the essentials," I say, hauling a cardboard box inside.

I set up the lamp I scavenged from my living room — a tacky thing with a pleated shade that casts a warm, amber glow over the yellow walls. I toss a navy blue pillow onto the floor where she'll be sleeping. I set a thrift-store ceramic mug on the galley counter, a chunky thing with a chipped rim. I don't say it, but I want her to have something that doesn't look like it belongs in a shelter.

We eat takeout from a bag of grease-stained paper. Two burgers, a styrofoam container of fish and grits, and two cans of lukewarm sweet tea.

Alex puts on a tape. The sound of distorted guitars fills the small room, heavy and sludge-slow. She sits on the floor, leaning her back against the wall, chewing her burger in silence.

The black case of Glitter sits on the counter.

It's right there. In plain sight. Every time Alex looks up, her eyes drift to it. She doesn't touch it. She doesn't even move toward it. She just watches it, the way a person watches a strange animal in a cage.

"You're gonna be fine," I say, leaning back against the counter. "Just follow the map. Don't get greedy."

She doesn't answer. She just listens to the music, the heavy chords vibrating through the floorboards.

Outside, a sodium streetlight flickers on, casting a rhythmic, orange pulse through the cracked blinds. A dog barks somewhere down the alley.

Alex eventually drifts off. She didn't bother with the pillow, just curled up on the stained carpet, her breathing shallow and steady. The dog tag pendant rests on the hollow of her chest, catching the amber light of my lamp.

Across the room, the black case remains on the counter, a small, dark rectangle against the yellow wall.
