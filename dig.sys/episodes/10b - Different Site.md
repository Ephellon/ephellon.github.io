# 10b – Different Site
**POV: Becky**

---

Alex's apartment smells like stale popcorn and the copper scent of the radiator. Diana's face is a blue-tinted ghost on the tablet screen, the brightness dialed low to keep the glare off the walls. The screen flickers, casting a pale light over the coffee table where a few stray eye-drop bottles roll in the periphery.

"The high school is the play," Diana says through the speakers, her voice thin and metallic. "East wing has the trophies. The visitors' center is a joke, but the pilgrims still stop there. It's a different flow than the house. Less congestion, but the marks are more cautious."

Roni leans against the wall, her shoulder blades against the peeling wallpaper. "I'll take the perimeter. I can see them coming from the main lot. I'll signal when the SUV pulls in."

Beth stands before the mirror, applying a second coat of lipstick. She presses her lips together and pulls them apart, checking the line in the glass. She turns her head left, then right, adjusting the angle. "We move the frame. Not the house, the legacy. The place where the 'Greatest Generation' of Blueville actually happened. It's not about where he slept; it's about where he learned to win."

Alex stays by the window. She stands with her back to the room, her palms flat against the glass. She watches the street below, her eyes tracking a slow-moving car that passes by.

The sun is a bleached white disc. We walk toward the high school, the pavement throwing off a heat that makes the air shimmer in waves over the asphalt. The crew moves in a loose line, with several feet of open asphalt between each person. We pass a row of shuttered storefronts with plywood over the windows and a parking lot filled with dead, yellow grass. The landscape is a series of gray rectangles and flat roofs. Beth keeps the compact mirror in her hand, clicking it shut, then opening it again. Her eyes move from the horizon to the mirror, then back to the horizon.

Blueville High is a sprawl of concrete and peeling beige paint. The architecture consists of interlocking, windowless blocks and corridors that run in straight, unbroken lines. Roni takes her spot by the gym fence. She stands with her arms crossed, shifting her weight from one foot to the other every few seconds. Her eyes move between the parking lot and the main entrance.

A family pulls up in a silver SUV. A couple and two kids. Their sneakers are crisp white. They step out and blink against the light. They look at the school, their heads tilting up to see the roofline.

Beth steps forward.

"You're looking for the real Blueville, right? Not the brochure version," she says. She pauses, then repeats the line, the words coming a little faster the second time. "The stuff they don't put on the placards. The grit."

The father looks at her. Beth does not look back at his eyes; she looks at the collar of his shirt, focusing on a small piece of lint.

"We can do a deep-dive tour," Beth says. "The locker rooms, the trophy case, the spots where he actually spent his time. The places the official tour avoids because they aren't curated."

The father asks the price.

"$20 per person," Beth says.

She stops speaking. The father nods and reaches for his wallet.

Beth leads them toward the east wing. The air inside the school is stagnant, smelling of old floor wax and damp cardboard. She stops at a smudge on the wall, a dark gray mark against the beige. She points to the mark. She tells them it is where the President's varsity jacket used to hang during the winter of '74. She describes a game from that year, mentioning a rainstorm that turned the field into a swamp and a missed field goal in the final three seconds.

"Right here," she says, gesturing to a patch of worn linoleum that has faded to a dull yellow. "This was the silent corner. This is where he'd hide out with the other boys to avoid the coach when they were skipping the film study."

She glances back at me and Alex every few minutes. Her eyes flick to my face, then to Alex, then back to the father. She leads them to a narrow alcove behind a set of lockers. She describes a "forbidden" cigarette spot, inventing a rivalry with the neighboring town that lasts ten minutes of walking. She says "imagine it" four times in the span of two blocks.

We follow them into the gym. The echoes of Beth's voice bounce off the high ceilings, ringing in the empty space. She leads them to a row of trophy cases where the gold-plated plastic has started to flake, leaving small metallic scales on the glass shelves. She points to a small, unremarkable plaque. She tells the family this was the moment the President realized he could lead, describing a speech he gave to the team after a loss—something about the "will of the Gulf Coast" and "unbreakable spirits." The family stands in a semi-circle, the kids looking at their phones while the parents nod.

Beth's voice gets louder. Her hands sweep from the floorboards up to the ceiling, tracing a wide arc in the air. She repeats the word "destiny" three times in one paragraph. She moves to the center of the court, her heels clicking on the hardwood, and describes the exact spot where the final buzzer sounded in the state championships. She stands on a specific floorboard, her weight shifting as she speaks.

Alex walks a few paces behind. Her eyes flick from the father's back pocket to Beth's open palm, then back to the wallet. She adjusts the strap of her bag, her fingers tapping a fast rhythm against the leather.

As the tour winds down, Beth leads them back toward the SUV. The father pulls out his wallet. He hands her four twenty-dollar bills.

$80.

The family thanks her. They get into their vehicle and drive away, the tires kicking up a cloud of gray dust from the lot.

A man in a tan cap sits in a folding chair by the curb. He has been there the whole time. He does not wave, and he does not yell. He watches the SUV leave, then shifts his gaze to Beth. He stays in the chair, his hands resting on his knees.

We start the walk back. The air is stagnant, the heat creating a blur over the asphalt. We count the money.

$80 net.

Beth does not speak. She keeps the money in her palm, the bills slightly damp. She looks at the ground, her steps falling a beat behind the rest of us. She walks with her chin down, her eyes fixed on the cracks in the sidewalk. When she moves her hand, the bills crinkle, but she does not look at them. She keeps her arms at her sides.
