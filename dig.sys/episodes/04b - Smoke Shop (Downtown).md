# 4b – Smoke Shop (Downtown)
**POV: Becky**

---

The heat is a wet blanket today. It clings to everything, smelling of salt and old asphalt. Downtown Blueville is a stretch of faded storefronts and desperation, currently clogged with the mid-summer trickle of presidential-pilgrim tourists. They wander around in breathable linens, buying cheap flags and overpriced brochures about the President's childhood, ignoring the salt-rot eating the bottoms of the buildings.

Alex is chewing her thumbnail. She looks small in her oversized shirt, her short brown hair sticking up in a dozen different directions. She keeps glancing at the screen of her phone—the glass is a spiderweb of cracks, the display flickering every time she swipes.

"You're gonna go blind staring at that thing," I say.

She doesn't answer. She just stares at a shop window. *PHONES — ALL MAKES — LICENSED.* The sign is peeling at the edges, yellowed by the sun. The window is streaked with a layer of grey dust that no one has bothered to wipe away.

"I need a new one," she says. "I can't... I can't do anything with this."

I shrug. I'm just walking beside her. Usually, I'm the one suggesting the shortcuts, the one pointing out the unlocked doors. But Alex has this look on her face—a kind of quiet, frantic resolve I don't recognize. She isn't asking me for a plan.

We go inside. The air conditioning is weak, barely fighting the humidity. The shop is small, smelling of ozone and stale tobacco. There are a few communication devices in dust-streaked cases—sleek new slabs and some older, modular kits. There are no other customers. Just one guy behind the counter. He's got a thick neck and a gaze that doesn't move from the door. He doesn't greet us. He doesn't even smile. He just watches.

Alex drifts toward the display cases. She's acting casual, but her breathing is shallow. I see her hand hover over a slim, black handheld.

She thinks he's looking at me. Or maybe she thinks he's just bored. She makes a quick, jerky motion—a slip of the wrist—and the device vanishes into her bag.

I see it happen. I open my mouth to tell her she's being an idiot, but the guy behind the counter has already moved. He doesn't shout. He doesn't jump over the counter. He just steps out, moving slow and heavy.

"That's a nice piece of hardware," he says. His voice is flat. "Too bad you can't afford it."

Alex freezes. She looks at him, her light grey eyes wide, her frame shaking. She doesn't try to lie. She doesn't try to run. She just stands there, clutching the strap of her bag.

"I... I can pay for it," she whispers.

The guy doesn't look at her bag. He looks at her face. "I'm sure you can. But we don't do payment plans here." He pauses. "Come with me. There's somebody you should meet."

He doesn't grab her. He doesn't have to. He just turns and walks toward the door, and Alex follows him like a dog on a leash. I follow a step behind, my stomach doing a slow roll. I want to say something, to tell him she's just a kid, but the look in the guy's eyes tells me that doesn't matter. This isn't a shop; it's a watch-post. We just walked into a fence.

It's a short walk. Only a block and a half of shimmering heat and cracked sidewalks. The guy walks between them, a silent shadow. Alex is staring at the pavement, her footsteps small and hesitant.

We stop at the Smoke Shop. The yellow signage is faded, the red awning sagging in the middle. The blue neon "OPEN" sign hums with a low, electric buzz.

Inside, Q is behind the counter. He's leaning back, reading a crumpled newspaper. He sees the employee walk in with two shaking girls. He doesn't look up. He doesn't say a word. He just turns a page, the sound of the paper crisp in the quiet shop. He's seen this a thousand times. To Q, we're just more inventory.

A second man is waiting near the back, leaning against a rack of novelty lighters. He's older — pushing fifty, gray creeping into his beard, a button-down shirt rolled to the forearms. He looks like a guy who played varsity linebacker fifteen years ago and never found a reason to stop acting like it.

"Put the phone on the counter," the man says.

Alex reaches into her bag and sets the device down. Her hand is trembling so hard the phone rattles against the glass.

The man looks at the phone, then at Alex. He doesn't look angry. He looks like an accountant reviewing a ledger.

"You tried to steal from us," he says. "That's a problem. We don't like problems."

"I'm sorry," Alex says. Her voice is small. "I'll do anything."

The man tilts his head. "Actually, that's the point. We have a problem of our own. A distribution problem. We need someone who looks like they don't belong in this life. Someone naive. Someone who can walk into places without drawing a second look."

He reaches into a drawer and pulls out a small, plastic case. Inside are several small vials of clear liquid with a shimmering, iridescent quality.

"Glitter," the man says. "Ocular Oxy. High demand, low overhead. You move this product, and the debt for the phone goes away. Then, you keep moving it, and we start paying you for your time."

"She's not—" I start, stepping forward.

The man doesn't even look at me. He just raises a hand, a slight gesture that cuts me off completely. He's talking to the asset, not the accessory.

"The math is simple," he says to Alex. "You work off the theft. You follow the instructions. You don't skim. If you do, we stop being business partners and we start being something else. Do you understand?"

Alex looks at the Glitter. Then she looks at the man. Her shoulders sag, a heavy, sudden stillness settling over her as she stares back at the iridescent vials.

"I understand," she says.

He slides the case across the counter toward her. "Good. We'll be in touch."

The walk back to my place is silent. The sun is dipping lower, casting long, orange shadows across the coastal strip. Alex is walking close to me, but she feels miles away. She's gone small, pulling her shoulders in, trying to disappear into her own skin. I try to think of something to say, some joke to break the tension, but the words feel heavy and useless. I can't fix this. I didn't set the trap, and I don't know how to spring it.

That night, Alex is on my couch. She isn't sleeping.

The room is dim, lit only by the streetlights filtering through the blinds. She's lying on her side, curled in a ball. Her hand is clenched tight around the dog tag pendant, the metal digging into her palm.

On the kitchen counter, just a few feet from the edge of the couch, the starter case of Glitter sits in the moonlight. The iridescent liquid catches the light, shimmering with a cold, artificial glow.

Alex stays awake, her grey eyes fixed on the case, watching it. She doesn't touch it.
