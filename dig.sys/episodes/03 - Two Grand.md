# 3 – Two Grand
**POV: Becky**

---

The air in the house is thick. It tastes of old Newport cigarettes and damp carpet. The blinds are pulled tight, cutting the afternoon sun into thin, yellow strips across the linoleum. It's the kind of dim that makes everything look like a Polaroid left in a glovebox too long.

Mom is standing by the kitchen table. She isn't shouting. Shouting takes energy, and she looks like she's run out of it years ago. She has her arms crossed, her face set in a flat, gray expression.

"Two thousand dollars, Alex," Mom says. Her voice is thin. "Two thousand dollars in an envelope, and now it's gone."

Alex stands in the center of the room. She's still wearing the oversized thrift-store shirt from the walk back from Perky's. She looks small. Smaller than usual.

"I didn't take it," Alex says. "I don't even know where the envelope was."

"Stop it. Just stop with the lying."

I lean against the doorframe, shifting my weight. I don't say anything. I just watch the way Alex's shoulders hunch, the way she starts to sway slightly from side to side. It's her tell. The wobble. She does it when she's trying to keep herself from shaking.

The boyfriend is on the couch. He's staring at a plate of lukewarm fries, poking one with a finger. He doesn't look up. He doesn't look at Alex. He's wearing a stained undershirt that shows how much he's let himself go. He knows. I can see it in the way he avoids the center of the room, the way he keeps his head down, huddled into the upholstery like he's trying to merge with the fabric. He doesn't speak. He doesn't even breathe loud enough to be noticed. He just sits there, a heavy, silent weight that makes the living room feel half its actual size.

"I didn't touch your money," Alex says. Her voice is higher now. Not a scream, but a plea. "Mom, I swear. I haven't even been in this room today."

Mom doesn't blink. She just looks at Alex with a tired kind of disgust. It's not the look of a parent who is angry; it's the look of a parent who has finally reached a conclusion.

"I can't do this anymore," Mom says. "I can't have this in my house. The stealing, the lying. I'm done."

Alex stops swaying. She goes completely still. "What does that mean?"

"It means you're gone," Mom says. "Pack your things. Get out."

The silence that follows is heavy. The hum of the refrigerator fills the gap. The boyfriend finally looks up, catches Alex's eye for a fraction of a second, and then looks back at his fries. He doesn't say a word. He doesn't defend her. He just keeps poking at the food, his movements slow and clumsy.

Alex doesn't move for a long time. Then, her hand goes to her neck. Her fingers find the dog tag pendant on its cord, brushing the metal, gripping it hard. She holds onto it like it's a handle she can use to pull herself back up.

"You're kicking me out?" Alex asks. "Over money I didn't take?"

"Go," Mom says. It isn't a command; it's a dismissal. She turns her back to Alex and starts picking up a few crumbs from the table with her palm.

Alex looks at the boyfriend. He is staring at the TV now, though the volume is muted. He is a ghost in a stained shirt.

Alex doesn't argue anymore. She doesn't fight. She just turns and walks toward her room. I follow her, staying a few paces back. I watch her grab a beat-up nylon backpack from the floor. She doesn't pack a suitcase. She doesn't take much — a few shirts, a charger, a notebook. She moves quickly, with a frantic, jerky precision.

She comes back out into the hallway. She's carrying the bag over one shoulder. She stops at the door and looks back at the kitchen.

Mom is still facing the wall. The boyfriend is still on the couch. Neither of them looks at her.

Alex walks past them. She doesn't say goodbye. She doesn't ask where she's supposed to go. She just steps through the front door and out onto the porch.

The humidity of the Gulf Coast hits us instantly, a wet blanket of heat and salt. I follow her out and close the door behind us. The sound of the latch clicking into place is quiet, but it sounds final.

Alex stands on the top step, her chest rising and falling in short, quick bursts. She's holding the strap of her bag so hard her knuckles are white. She doesn't look at me. She looks out at the street, where the asphalt is shimmering in the heat.

She lets go of the bag. She lets go of the dog tag. She just stands there in the fading light.

"Come on," I say.

She doesn't answer. She just starts walking down the driveway.
