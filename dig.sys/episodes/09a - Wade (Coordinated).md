
# 9a — Wade (Coordinated)
**POV: Becky**

---

The sun has shifted, dragging the shadows of the dilapidated storefronts across the cracked asphalt. It is still hot, the kind of heat that makes the air taste like old pennies and exhaust, but the peak of the afternoon is passing.

Beth is in the home stretch. She is leaning into the performance, her voice honey-thick, gesturing toward a concrete culvert where the dry creek
bed begins. The pilgrim family is following her lead, the two children looking half-melted in their synthetic t-shirts.

"And here," Beth says, pausing and pointing a manicured nail toward a rusted iron pipe jutting from the silt, "is where the President's father reportedly kept his 'secret' fishing tackle. Local legend says the young man spent three summers here, learning the patience required for politics by failing to catch a single Blueville catfish."

The mother smiles and holds up her phone, snapping a photo of the muddy pipe. The father nods, looking impressed by the sheer specificity of the lie. He likes the "authentic" grit of it. He likes feeling like he's seen something the official brochures missed.

"I appreciate it, Bethany," the father says. He reaches out and shakes Beth's hand. "The real thing. That's what we wanted. Truly a local experience."

He steps back, gathering the kids, and they head back toward the main road. I watch Beth's hand slide into her back pocket, feeling the crispness of the eighty dollars. I can practically hear the bills snapping
against her hip.

Wade doesn't move until the family is around the corner.

He's been there the whole time, leaning against a rusted chain-link fence across the street. He doesn't rush. He pushes off the metal with a slow, practiced motion, his movements casual, almost lazy. He is wearing a Blueville High varsity jacket that has faded from a deep navy to something
closer to a bruised plum. The wool is pilled, the leather sleeves cracked in the sun. In this town, that jacket isn't clothing; it's a deed to a piece of land he can't actually own.

He walks over at a steady pace. Roni is still twenty yards back, leaning on a lamp post, her eyes tracking the perimeter, but she stays put.

"Bethany Miller," Wade says.

He doesn't smile. He doesn't look angry. He just looks like a man who has spent a decade watching the same four blocks of pavement.

Alex looks at him, blinking, her expression blank. She doesn't recognize him. I don't recognize him either, but I recognize the type. He's the local ghost, the one who knows exactly which cracks in the sidewalk are the deepest.

"You're on my turf, Miller," he says. "I've got a list. Those four were on
my list. I was going to hit them the second they finished at the marker."

Beth doesn't flinch. She doesn't even look surprised. She shifts her weight, her face softening into a look of sudden, shimmering recognition.

"Wade? Wade Jenkins?" she asks. She lets out a small, airy laugh. "I haven't seen you since... God, what was it? You were a sophomore when I was a senior. You used to sit on Coach Higgins' bench during the varsity games, didn't you? I remember you always had those oversized cleats."

Wade stops. The tension in his shoulders doesn't vanish, but it changes. He looks at her, really looks at her, searching for the girl he remembered
from the hallways a decade ago. Beth is playing him perfectly, projecting a version of herself that belongs to this street, to this town, to the same dying memory he's clinging to.

"Higgins hated those shoes," Wade says. His voice is flatter now, less an accusation and more a conversation.

"He hated everything," Beth replies. "Some things never change."

Wade shifts his weight. He's not won over, but he's decided she isn't an interloper. She's a local coming home to bleed the tourists. That's a language he speaks.

"I don't like people poaching my marks," Wade says. "But I don't like fighting people I know."

"I'm not poaching, Wade. I'm diversifying," Beth says. She tilts her head.
"How about a partnership? You've got the eyes. You know who's coming in with deep pockets and who's just here for the free parking. You tell me which families are worth the effort, and I'll tell you which ones are too stingy to bother with."

Wade looks at the road, then back at her. "I want a cut of today's take. Just for the peace of mind."

"I can do that," Beth says. "But I'm not giving you half. Twenty dollars from this gig, and we start the info-sharing on the next one. Deal?"

Wade doesn't hesitate. "Twenty dollars. Deal."

Beth reaches into her back pocket. She pulls out a twenty and holds it out. Wade takes it without ceremony, sliding the bill into the pocket of the varsity jacket.

"See you around, Miller," he says. He gives a short, sharp nod and walks back toward the fence, his sleeves pushed up against the heat, disappearing into the glare of the afternoon.

Roni walks up to us then. She's quiet, as usual. She saw the whole thing from her post, her face a mask of professional indifference.

"We good?" Roni asks.

"We're great," Beth says, her energy returning, the "on" switch still flipped. "We've got a scout."

Beth pulls the remaining money out. She fans it out in her palm, counting it with a quick, practiced thumb.

"Eighty gross," she says. "Twenty to the local. Sixty net."

I look at Alex.

She isn't listening to Beth's plan for the next tour. She isn't looking at
the street or the crumbling buildings. She is watching the money. Her eyes
are fixed on the bills in Beth's hand, tracking the movement of the paper,
the way the edges curl. It is the same way she watched the father hand over the cash earlier.

"Sixty bucks," Beth says, grinning. "It's a start."

We start walking back. Beth is talking fast now, energized, sketching out a route that takes the pilgrims past the old cinema and the defunct bottling plant. Roni is beside her, asking about logistics, timing, and exit points.

Alex falls a few steps behind. I watch her hand drift up to the dog tag pendant at her throat, her fingers brushing the metal. She doesn't say anything. She just keeps her eyes on Beth's hand, watching the sixty dollars shift as Beth gestures with them, the cash flickering in the sunlight.
