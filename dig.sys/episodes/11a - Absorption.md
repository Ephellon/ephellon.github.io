The air at eleven in the morning smells of hot asphalt and damp earth. Across the street, the cicadas are screaming in the oaks, a high, vibrating wall of noise that fills the gaps between breaths, shimmering in 
the glare of the midday sun. 

Wade is already there. He is leaning against a white clapboard building, a 
small church with peeling paint on the eaves and a single, narrow steeple that looks like it might lean if the wind picked up. He wears the faded varsity jacket despite the temperature, the heavy wool a dull gold in the light. He doesn't look hot; he just looks like part of the architecture.

A single SUV, dark blue and polished to a mirror finish, sits in the shade 
of a sprawling magnolia. The leaves are thick, casting a deep, cool shadow 
over the hood of the car.

Roni takes a spot by the edge of the sidewalk, leaning back with her arms crossed, eyes scanning the quiet residential street. She doesn't move, just shifts her weight every few minutes, tracking the few cars that pass through. Diana is beside her, thumb tapping a rhythmic, mindless pattern on the glass of her tablet. Beth is the only one moving, stepping toward Wade with a look of professional curiosity. Alex follows, her pace steady, 
her face held in that same fixed, flat expression from the historical marker. Her hand is hooked around the dog tag pendant, fingers brushing the metal.

Wade doesn't greet them. He doesn't smile. He looks at them as a group, his eyes moving from Roni to Diana, then Beth, and finally pausing on Alex. He speaks in a flat, list-like cadence, as if reading from a ledger he has memorized.

"New terms," Wade says. "Pre-approved scripts. Pre-vetted pilgrims only. No street pickups, no randoms. You follow the framework, you don't deviate. My cut goes to twenty-five percent on these gigs."

He doesn't pause for a reaction. He doesn't ask if they agree. He just stands there in the heat, waiting.

Roni nods once. Diana continues to tap the tablet, her eyes not leaving the screen. Beth looks at the church and then back at Wade.

"Okay," Beth says.

The SUV door opens. A man in his late fifties steps out. He is wearing linen pants and a crisp white shirt. On his wrist is a watch with a brown leather band and a polished gold case. He moves with a deliberate, slow grace, his voice soft when he greets Wade. He doesn't look at the crew at first, only at Wade, a brief exchange of nods between two people who already know the price of the day.

The man doesn't ask questions. He doesn't look for a brochure or a map. He 
reaches into his pocket and pulls out four hundred dollars in cash. He hands it to Wade before the tour even begins.

Wade takes the money and nods toward the church.

Inside, the air is cooler but smells of old wax and damp wool. Dust motes drift through the shafts of light cutting across the pews, gold and heavy. 
The floorboards creak under their feet, a slow, rhythmic sound that echoes 
in the small sanctuary. The pews are dark oak, scarred with a century of small scratches and worn smooth at the edges where people have slid in and 
out. Beth begins the pitch. This isn't the scandal-heavy routine she used at the marker. There is no talk of buried-water or political theater. Under Wade's framework, the tone is different.

"He didn't just come here for the pews," Beth says, her voice echoing slightly in the small room. "He came for the quiet. The kind of quiet that 
builds a man. He spent his youth here learning the art of preparation. Discipline. The ability to sit in a room and wait for the right moment to move."

The pilgrim nods, his eyes tracing the simple wooden cross at the front of 
the room. He stands still, his hands clasped behind his back, listening to 
the words as if they are part of the architecture.

Alex moves. She doesn't look at me, and she doesn't look at Beth. She speaks, her voice cutting through the air with a smoothness that doesn't stumble.

"The kind of quiet that makes you hear things other people miss," Alex says.

The line fits perfectly into the rhythm of Beth's speech. The pilgrim stops walking. He turns to look at Alex. He nods, slowly, and looks back at the altar.

Beth glances at Alex. She doesn't pause the pitch. She doesn't ask where the line came from. She just continues, weaving the moment into the narrative as they lead the man back toward the door.

The tour ends ten minutes later. The pilgrim steps back into the SUV and pulls away from the curb without a word, the tires humming against the asphalt.

Wade is waiting on the sidewalk. He doesn't ask how it went. He reaches into his pocket, pulls out a hundred dollars, and pockets it without ceremony. He doesn't say goodbye. He doesn't offer a critique. He just turns and walks away, the varsity jacket disappearing around the corner of 
the clapboard building.

Beth counts out the remaining three hundred. She splits it among the crew.

We start the walk back. Roni is humming something low and discordant. Diana is finally putting the tablet away.

I watch Alex. She is walking three paces behind the others. Her hand is still gripped around the dog tag pendant, her knuckles pale. She isn't looking at the trees or the houses we pass. Her eyes are fixed on the concrete.

She is stepping precisely on the seams of the sidewalk. One foot on the line, then the other. She doesn't deviate. She doesn't adjust her stride to avoid the cracks or the uneven patches of grey. She just keeps the rhythm, stepping exactly on the thin, dark lines of the pavement, her face 
as still as the church we just left.
