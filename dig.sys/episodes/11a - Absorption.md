
# 11a – Absorption
**POV: Becky**

---

Across the street, the cicadas are screaming in the canopy of the oaks, a high, metallic drone that fills every gap in the conversation. The street is quiet, residential, the kind of place where the lawns are manicured but the paint is peeling in long, gray strips.

Wade is already there. He is leaning against a white fence, wearing the faded varsity jacket. The heavy fabric of the jacket remains dry despite the humidity. He doesn't move. He just waits.

The church is a small clapboard building, white and narrow, with a steep pitch to the roof. White paint on the clapboards is fresh on the trim but bubbling in long strips near the foundation. The windows are clear, the glass wavy in a way that distorts the trees across the street. A single SUV, black and shimmering with heat, is parked in the only patch of shade provided by a dying elm.

Roni takes the perimeter, shifting her weight from side to side, eyes scanning the end of the block. Diana has her tablet out, the screen flickering in the glare of the sun. Beth is standing next to Alex. Alex is 
quiet. Her hand is resting near the dog tag pendant, her fingers tracing the edge of the metal.

Wade doesn't greet them. He doesn't smile. He looks at the crew and speaks 
in a flat, list-like cadence.

"Pre-approved script," Wade says. "Pre-vetted pilgrims only. No street pickups. My cut grows to twenty-five percent on these."

A silence follows. It stretches, filled only by the drone of the cicadas and the distant sound of a lawnmower two streets over. Roni nods. Diana taps a finger against the glass of her tablet. Beth looks at Wade, then at 
the SUV, then back to Wade. She doesn't ask about the percentage. She doesn't ask how the vetting works. 

"Okay," Beth says.

The crew settles into a waiting pattern. We stand in a loose semi-circle, the heat pressing into our backs. There is no negotiation, no questioning of the new math. We just wait for the signal.

The man from the SUV pulls the door shut. He is in his late fifties. He wears linen pants and a white shirt with the sleeves rolled up. A watch with a leather band sits on his left wrist. He doesn't look like the kind of man who flaunts money, but he moves with a slow, deliberate confidence. 
He doesn't look at the crew. He looks at Wade.

"Good morning," the man says. His voice is soft, coming from deep in his chest.

"Good morning," Wade says.

The man reaches into his pocket and pulls out a fold of bills. He hands the cash to Wade. Four hundred dollars. No counting, no hesitation. He pays for the access before the tour even begins.

Wade steps aside and gestures toward the church. "Beth."

Beth steps forward. Her posture changes. The sharp, hustler edge from the historical marker is gone. She slows her voice down, adding a layer of reverence that sounds like a Sunday school teacher.

"Weâ€™ll start with the sanctuary," Beth says, leading the man toward the clapboard door. "This is where the President developed his sense of preparation. He didn't just attend; he studied the architecture of the service. The discipline of the long hour."

The interior of the church smells of lemon oil and old, damp paper. Dust motes drift in the shafts of light cutting through the high windows. The pews are narrow, dark wood polished to a mirror finish by decades of shifting bodies.

The man walks slowly, his leather shoes clicking on the floor. He stops in 
the center aisle, looking up at the vaulted ceiling. He reaches out and brushes a finger against the back of a pew, checking the grain of the wood.

"He spent a lot of time in the front row," Beth says, her voice echoing slightly. "He understood that visibility is a tool. If you are seen in the 
right place, at the right time, the story writes itself."

The man looks at the altar, then back at Beth. "And his family? Were they as disciplined?"

"The family provided the foundation," Beth says. "The quiet support that allowed the discipline to take root. Everything was focused on the eventual ascent."

They move toward the vestry, a cramped room off the main hall with a single wooden chair and a small side table. The air here is heavier, smelling of stale dust and cold stone. A small, weathered lectern sits in the corner, the wood scarred with old ink stains. The man stops and looks at the chair, then turns to Beth.

"Was he always this quiet?" the man asks.

"The silence was a choice," Beth says, her voice steady and sure. "He practiced a kind of calculated stillness. He learned that the man who speaks the least often controls the room."

Alex is standing a few paces behind them. She hasn't spoken since we left the house. She is just there, a shadow in an oversized shirt.

As the man turns to look at the lectern, Alex speaks.

"He didn't have a desk back then," Alex says. "Just a pine table and a lamp."

The man stops. He looks at Alex, his head tilting slightly. He doesn't ask 
who she is. He holds her gaze for a second, then nods once, slowly.

Beth glances at Alex. She doesn't pause. She doesn't ask how Alex knew that. She just continues the tour, moving back toward the sanctuary door, her voice returning to the script of discipline and preparation.

We follow them through the small building, through the rooms that smell of 
lemon oil and old paper. The man asks a few more questions in a low murmur. Beth answers them with the approved precision.

When the tour ends, the man walks back to the SUV. He doesn't say goodbye. 
He just closes the door and pulls away from the curb, leaving a cloud of exhaust and the scent of hot asphalt.

Wade steps forward. He reaches into his pocket and pulls out a hundred dollars. He doesn't count it. He just pockets the money without ceremony.

He turns and walks away, heading back toward the main road without a word to any of us.

Beth hands the remaining three hundred to Diana. Diana splits it.

We start the walk back. The sun is at its peak now, the heat shimmering off the road in wavy lines. Roni and Diana are talking about where to eat, 
their voices low and routine, debating between a taco truck and a deli. Beth is checking her phone, her thumb scrolling rapidly through a feed.

Alex walks beside me. Her hand is back at the dog tag pendant, fingers brushing the metal.

She is walking in a straight line, her eyes locked on a point three inches 
above the horizon. She doesn't look at the houses. She doesn't look at the 
trees. She doesn't blink. Her stride is rhythmic and mechanical, never changing pace, even when the sidewalk dips or the pavement cracks.

I watch her profile. I watch the way her gaze never wavers from that empty 
point in the air.

She keeps walking.
