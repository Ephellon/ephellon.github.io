
# 12b — Closed Door
<strong class="meta-line">POV  &bull;  Becky</strong>

---

Beth isn't with us. She wasn't in the apartment when we left, and there was no note on the counter. We don't talk about it. We just walk.

Roni leads the way, her pace steady and fast. Diana and I follow, and Alex 
is tucked in between us. The sun is a white glare on the pavement, turning 
the asphalt into a shimmering haze. It is Day 6. In the oaks, the cicadas are screaming, a high, mechanical drone that fills the silence between our 
steps. The heat sits on the skin, unmoving.

We reach the residential block. The white fence is still there, the paint peeling in long, curling strips that flutter in the slight breeze. The elm 
tree has grey, peeling bark and bare branches. Across the street, the clapboard church stands still, its wavy glass windows reflecting the glare 
of the mid-morning sun.

Wade is at his post. He is leaning against the fence in that faded varsity 
jacket, his weight shifted to one hip. He holds a paper cup in his right hand, sipping slowly. He doesn't move when he sees us coming. He doesn't stand up straighter or shift his stance to greet us. He watches us approach, his gaze flat and unblinking.

Roni stops a few feet from him. Diana and I come to a halt behind her. Alex doesn't look at Wade's face. She looks at the frayed cuff of his jacket, then her gaze drifts to the empty stretch of fence line beyond him, where the shade-patch is vacant. Her hand is at her neck, her thumb rubbing the edge of the dog tag.

Roni doesn't waste time with greetings.

"Is this permanent?" she asks.

Wade doesn't look at her. He looks past her, out toward the church and the 
empty street. He takes a slow sip from the cup, the plastic lid making a small, wet sound.

"Different operation," he says.

He waits a beat, his eyes remaining on the horizon.

"Not your block."

He takes another sip, then looks at Roni.

"Take care of yourselves."

There is no shrug, no tilt of the head. He doesn't mention the man in the tan cap. He doesn't mention the name of the person who gave the order. He just 
leans there, the varsity jacket looking too heavy for the heat.

Roni doesn't ask why. She doesn't ask about the intercepted pilgrim or the 
shift in the stream. She stays still for a moment, eyes fixed on Wade, then she turns.

We follow her. We walk in a loose line, the sound of our shoes on the grit 
the only thing competing with the cicadas. The walk back feels longer than 
the walk there. The smell of hot asphalt and dying grass hangs in the air. 
Sweat makes the fabric of my shirt cling to my back.

Roni walks a few paces ahead of us. She keeps a constant, fast stride and does not look back to see if we're keeping up. She doesn't say a word. Her 
gaze is fixed on the road ahead, never drifting to the sides. As we reach the turn, I catch her face for a half-second. Her eyes are still. They aren't scanning or drifting; they aren't moving.

We get back to the apartment. The air inside is stale and smells of old dust and unwashed linens. We move through the entryway in silence, the floorboards creaking under our weight.

The door closes behind us.
