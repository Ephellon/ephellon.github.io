# 1 – Alex
**POV: Becky**

---

The smell of old frying oil and lemon-scented bleach clings to everything in Perky’s. It’s a heavy, wet smell. Ma is leaning on the Formica counter, her arms crossed, looking at Alex.

Alex is holding a tray of half-empty water glasses. She’s standing too still, her shoulders hunched, looking like she’s trying to disappear into her oversized uniform shirt. The shirt is two sizes too big. It swallows her.

"You're a good girl, Alex," Ma says. "But you're just not a waitress."

Alex doesn't say anything. She looks at the glasses.

"You've dropped three carafes this week," Ma says. "And the table in the corner said you forgot their order twice. I can't do it. Not with the dinner rush coming."

I'm leaning against the jukebox, picking at a piece of chipped red paint. I know the look on Alex's face. It’s the look of someone who just realized they’re in the way. She isn't sad. She's just blank.

"And you," Ma says, shifting her gaze to me.

I stop picking at the paint. I don't blink.

"My son is on a full ride to State, Becky. A full ride. And he can't focus on his finals because he's too busy worrying about where you're going to end up next."

I shrug. It's a small movement. I can see Caleb sitting in the far booth with the others. He won't look at me. He's staring at his phone, pretending the air between us isn't vibrating.

"You're both done," Ma says. "Leave the aprons on the counter."

We walk over to the booth. The group is already there.

"Told you it wouldn't last," says Marcus, leaning back with his arms spread across the vinyl seat. He’s wearing a varsity jacket from a school he graduated five years ago. In Blueville, that jacket is a permanent residence.

Alex slides into the booth, the apron still tied around her waist. She looks small.

"It wasn't that bad," Alex says. Her voice is thin.

"You tried to serve a plate of grits to a man who had already left the building, Al," Marcus says. He's grinning.

"Remember the eighth-grade talent show?" someone else asks.

"The robot," Marcus says. "She spent three months building that damn thing out of old toaster parts and a discarded motherboard from the library. She thought it would walk across the stage and hand the principal a flower."

Alex lets out a short, dry sound. "The motor burned out."

"Burned out?" Marcus looks at the others. "It exploded. It took out the front row of the choir. I still remember the look on the principal's face. Alex just stood there with her goggles on."

I watch her. She’s smiling, but it doesn't reach her eyes. She stares at the table, her thumb tracing a circle in the spilled salt, a small, distant look on her face. That’s the Alex I know. The one who tries too hard at things that don't matter.

"Your mom's gonna love this," I say.

Alex’s smile fades. "She probably won't care. She's mostly just happy the house is quiet when I'm at work."

Her mom is still there, still picking her boyfriend over her daughter, still pretending the house doesn't smell like stale cigarettes.

"You could actually go back for the GED, Alex," says Sarah. "The center is taking new students. You're smart. Smarter than all of us."

Alex looks at the table. She doesn't answer.

"She's not going back to school," I say. "She's too busy being a digital sissy."

Alex gives me a look. A mix of affection and a desire to punch me in the arm. She reaches into her pocket and taps the screen of her phone, a small, sharp movement.

The group keeps talking, the conversation drifting back to the football games and the parties at the lake. It's a loop. A circle. We're all just orbiting the same dead center.

I stand up and reach over, untying the strings of Alex's apron. She doesn't move. I pull the fabric away from her waist and toss it onto the table. It lands with a wet thud, stained with grease and a bit of spilled gravy.

"Come on," I say. "Let's go find something to do."

Alex stands up slowly. She looks at the apron, then at Ma, who is already scrubbing the counter.

Alex follows me out the door. The evening air is warm, smelling of salt and distant exhaust. She walks with her hands in her pockets, her shoulders still hunched.

She stops by the curb and looks back at the neon sign of Perky's, flickering in the twilight.

She turns away and starts walking.
