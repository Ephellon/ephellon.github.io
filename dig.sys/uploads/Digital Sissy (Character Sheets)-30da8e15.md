# Character Sheets — Digital Sissy

Designed for **Claude Design** to use across branding, prints, frontend UI/UX, and player-facing materials. Each sheet covers personality, story function, voice/manner, and visual or branding cues.

Visual canon (faces, builds, signature wardrobe) lives in the **Visual Style Guide** and Sora portrait library. This document is about who these people *are*, not what they look like.

**v0.3 changes:** Reorganized into three sections — Major NPCs, Minor NPCs, PCs. Added stub sheets for the seven archetype pairs not previously detailed. Sam canon updated to note both prose render (nonbinary they/them) and portrait render (male).

**v0.2 changes (preserved):** Cindy is Alex's mother (formerly unnamed "Mom"). Steve is Cindy's boyfriend (formerly unnamed "Mom's Boyfriend"). The clinic worker NPC previously called Cindy is now Carla, holding the Cleric archetype's female slot.

## Major NPCs

The seven characters with the heaviest narrative load. Alex and Becky lead — they're not technically PCs (players don't pick them), but they're protagonist and narrator respectively, so they belong here rather than the lighter Minor section.

---

### Alex (Alexandria)

**Identity:** Protagonist. 20s, female-presenting but androgynous-leaning, working-class Gulf Coast Alabama. Not a playable slot — players orbit around her trying to save her.

**Personality:** Naive in a specific way — not stupid, not sheltered, but unable to recognize a manipulation until it's already in motion. Loyal to a fault. Trusts Becky absolutely. Trusts adults less. Has a quiet stubbornness underneath the softness; once she decides to do something, she doesn't waver. Quick to apologize, slow to anger, but the anger when it comes is cold and specific. Carries her absent father's taste in metal as identity — it's the loudest thing about her. Online handle `Xph0n3__punkX` is her armor.

**Story function:** The center of the gravitational collapse. Every other character orbits her — the people who want to save her, the people who use her, the people who watch her go. Her death is the canonical climax. Her survival is the Pyrrhic save. Either way, what happens to her is the spine of the story.

**Voice/manner:** Speaks softly, even in arguments. Doesn't curse much. Sentences trail off rather than land. When she's confident, she's brief; when she's overwhelmed, she's even briefer.

**Branding cues:** Dog tag pendant (load-bearing recurring motif). Metalhead aesthetic but understated — not full battle vest. Light grey eyes that should feel slightly unfocused even in close-up. Iconography: pendant, headphones, vials. Online: glitchy 2000s-internet typography for `Xph0n3__punkX`-coded materials.

---

### Becky

**Identity:** Narrator (the DM voices her). Same age as Alex, childhood friend, lifelong bad influence. Not a playable slot.

**Personality:** Street-smart in the way that's actually shallow — knows the corners of Blueville, doesn't know the world. Dry, observant, performatively unbothered. Has loved Alex *and* ruined her in parallel since middle school; both parts are real. The kind of friend who will steal a car for you and also be the reason you needed to steal it. Knows herself well enough to be unsettled by her self-knowledge, not well enough to change. Loyal — but loyalty in her register means *being there*, not protecting.

**Story function:** Witness. The story is told through her eyes; she sees what we see. Survives by default but the campaign ends if she dies — she's narrator-load-bearing. In the canonical tragic ending, she's present at Alex's death but doesn't die there. She lives carrying it.

**Voice/manner:** Casual cadence, fragments, dry observations. Calls Alex "Al" informally. Curses with rhythm rather than emphasis. Funniest at the worst moments, quietest at the most ordinary ones.

**Branding cues:** Acrylic nails, jewelry, Valley Girl-aesthetic adjacent — but specifically the Gulf Coast working-class version of that look. The "Babygirl" necklace is canonical. Crop tops, jean shorts. Iconography: chipped nails, costume jewelry, phone screen. The visual register is "girl who has been the bad influence in every photo since age nine."

### Jacob "Jaw-man"

**Identity:** 30s, white, working-class Gulf Coast origin. The local King Pin of the Glitter trade. The cold-eyed mirror of who Alex becomes if she lives.

**Personality:** Carrying it. Not psychopathic — exhausted. Was a high school football star until a helmet accident broke his jaw and the lifeline that came with it. Painkillers became the trade became the empire. He has done every job he asks his runners to do. He doesn't enjoy any of this. He answers to people above him whose names he doesn't give. He is not the villain because he likes being the villain; he is the villain because there is no other shape his life has been allowed to take.

**Story function:** Catalyst and mirror. Provides the down payment that hooks Alex; delivers the $10K ultimatum that creates the campaign clock. Late-act revelation that there is structure above him deepens the world's coldness. His weariness toward Alex is the project's emotional center for *what this trade does to people* — he is the future she's running toward.

**Voice/manner:** Plainspoken, brief, never theatrical. Says exactly what needs to be said and stops. Looks at people while they speak; doesn't interrupt. Almost-says-things and doesn't.

**Branding cues:** Dark fitted polo (consistent uniform — slight variations in color), matte black watch, scarred knuckles. Habsburg-style mandibular prognathism (asymmetric jaw, lower jaw projects). Black Silverado. Iconography: the truck, the watch, the offset jaw silhouette. Visual register is "small-town middle management who happens to deal drugs" — corporate quiet, not cartel theatrical.

---

### Ted

**Identity:** 30s, male, high school acquaintance Alex never spoke to. Rival dealer. Robs Alex (Ep 6). Kills her at the canonical climax.

**Personality:** Forgettable specifically. The kind of guy who was always *around* in high school but never anyone's friend or anyone's enemy. Now: a bottom-feeder, mean when desperate. Not strategic, not stupid — just *small*, in a way that makes him dangerous because he has nothing to lose and no plan to protect. Operates on the edges of Jaw-man's territory.

**Story function:** The hand that does the things. Rob in Ep 6, kill in the climax. He is not the architect of Alex's death; he is the instrument. The architect is the system. Ted is the personalized face the system wears when it finally reaches her. Recognition-without-relationship — they went to school together but he is a stranger.

**Voice/manner:** Flat affect. Says "stay safe" after robbing her — banalities. Doesn't monologue. Looks like a guy finishing a chore. The lack of menace IS the menace.

**Branding cues:** A face you'd swear you know from somewhere — a gas station, a grocery store. Identifying mark: small faded blue ink blotch on the right wrist (Becky's recognition cue). Mid-thirties, average everything, thrift-store windbreaker. Iconography: the wrist tattoo, the folding knife. Visual register is "the guy who could be six different guys in your high school yearbook."

---

### Q (Quentin)

**Identity:** 60s+, Smoke Shop owner. Long-time fixture in Blueville. NPC.

**Personality:** Knows everything that happens in his zip code, says nothing about any of it. Has been the shop owner so long that he has watched two or three generations of dealers move through his back booth. Doesn't moralize — he runs a business that happens to be where the trade meets the street. Soft-eyed in a way that has nothing to do with being soft. Minds his business when it counts. Will do small kindnesses but never the big ones.

**Story function:** Hub. The Smoke Shop is the central convergence point for all three branches; Q is the man who runs that hub. Mechanically and narratively, he's the constant — when the players come to the Smoke Shop, Q is there. His non-reaction to events is itself information. Has not yet had a load-bearing moment, but is a candidate for a late-act beat (he sees the climax coming, says nothing, lets it happen — or breaks his pattern at the last moment).

**Voice/manner:** Short sentences when he speaks. Often doesn't speak at all. Nods at people he recognizes; doesn't nod at people he doesn't, even if they're in the room.

**Branding cues:** Weathered, "carved out of driftwood." Behind the counter with a rag or a newspaper or a small screen. Iconography: the counter, the faded yellow Smoke Shop exterior, the blue OPEN sign, the red awning (the most palette-saturated location in town is *his* shop). Visual register is "the old man who has seen it all and will not tell you any of it."

### Cindy

**Identity:** 40s, Alex's mother. Peaked in high school. Struggling parent.

**Personality:** Tired in the kind of way that doesn't go away with sleep. Chose Steve over Alex when forced to choose; doesn't know she made the wrong choice and will likely never know. Sympathetic, failing, not a villain. Wears a volleyball t-shirt from a high school team twenty years ago. Loves Alex but cannot afford to. Practices small cruelties without recognizing them as cruelties because she has received worse.

**Story function:** The eviction is the inciting incident — she sets the entire spiral in motion by accusing Alex of theft she didn't commit. She does not learn the truth that Steve took the money (canonical). Becomes peripheral after Ep 3; her absence from Alex's late life is part of the tragedy.

**Voice/manner:** Voice goes thin rather than loud. Doesn't shout. Walks away from arguments. "I can't do this anymore" is her register — exhausted ultimatum rather than enraged confrontation.

**Branding cues:** Oversized old-school-team t-shirts, sweatpants or denim shorts, hair pulled back without care. Iconography: the team t-shirt, the stack of mail, the phone pressed to her ear during conversations she doesn't end. Visual register is "the woman who was prom queen and got stuck."

**Note on prose usage:** Becky's POV calls her "Mom" or "Alex's mom" naturally (not "Cindy"). The name "Cindy" lives in the cast canon and in dialogue when other characters address her, but does not appear in Becky's narration except where context demands.

### Steve

**Identity:** 40s, Cindy's live-in partner. Warehouse job. Glitter user. **He stole the $2k.** Truth never surfaces to Cindy.

**Personality:** Peaked in high school harder than Cindy did. Coward, in the specific way of men who know they're cowards. Aware of his own failures and unwilling to face them. Becomes Alex's best customer post-Ep 5 — the man who got her kicked out is now buying drugs from her. He is sheepish about it. Alex extorts him via premium pricing; he pays. The arrangement is a small private violence neither of them names.

**Story function:** The hidden engine of the inciting incident — the actual thief who never confesses. Provides quiet ongoing canon-coverage that the system Alex got pulled into is not even built on a real crime. She lost everything over a lie someone close to her told.

**Voice/manner:** Doesn't speak when he should. Mumbles when he does. Avoids eye contact. His silence in Ep 3 is his canonical character beat — he could exonerate Alex with a sentence and doesn't.

**Branding cues:** Stained undershirt, recliner, lukewarm fries. Iconography: the recliner, the food he's poking at, the muted TV. Visual register is "the guy who used to be the guy and is now the man in the corner."

**Note on prose usage:** Becky's POV calls him "Steve," "the boyfriend," or "Cindy's boyfriend" depending on context. Becky has known him since the boyfriend moved in; she'd use his first name naturally in some moments and "the boyfriend" in others (especially when she's emphasizing his role over his personhood).

## Minor NPCs

Named characters with limited on-page presence. Tracked for canon continuity rather than branding depth.

---

### Dad

**Identity:** Alive but peripheral. Alex's absent father. Displaced by Steve years ago.

**Personality:** Largely off-page. What we know: gave Alex the dog tag pendant, gave Alex her metal taste. Not present in her life through this story. Whether he tried to be is canon-ambiguous and probably stays that way.

**Story function:** The pendant. He is, functionally, the pendant — that's how he exists in the story. Whenever Alex grips it, he is there. Whenever she lets it go, he is gone again. The structure of his absence is the structure of her vulnerability.

**Voice/manner:** Not present. Does not speak in this story.

**Branding cues:** A negative space. If the Designer needs an image of him at all, it should be implied — the pendant, an old metal-band poster, a denim jacket on a hook somewhere. Iconography: the pendant, by itself. Visual register is "the man whose absence furniture the room."

---

### Marcus

**Identity:** 20s, Blueville local. Recurring background character. Becky knows him from school.

**Personality:** Has not let go of high school in any meaningful way. Wears his old varsity jacket like it's load-bearing. Generally harmless but emblematic — the project's living embodiment of the "everyone peaks in high school" rule. Has appeared at Perky's (Ep 1) and at T-Bone's smoke session (Ep 4c). May appear again at the kind of places people end up when they don't leave town.

**Story function:** Ambient continuity. When a scene needs a recognizable Blueville face to anchor a setting, Marcus is the right reference. His repeat appearances create the sense of a small town where the same people show up across years without changing.

**Voice/manner:** Bro-y but not aggressive. Talks about high school games like they happened last week. Doesn't pick up on social cues that he should grow up.

**Branding cues:** Varsity jacket from Blueville High, five years out of date by Becky's reckoning. Beat-up sneakers. Iconography: the jacket. **Becky's canonical line about him: "In Blueville, that jacket is a permanent residence."** Visual register is the project's working thematic — a person who has become a museum exhibit of his own teenage years.

## PCs

Twelve archetype pairs. Each slot has an M/F variant. Players pick a slot for their campaign character; the unselected variant typically becomes an NPC in the canonical telling if needed. Of the twelve slots, five have been canonically rendered in prose so far (Bard-F as Beth, Cleric-F as Carla, Druid-F as Diana, Rogue-F as Roni, Sorcerer-M as Sam-NB). The remaining seven slots have only appeared in the group portrait so far.

All PCs are working-class Gulf Coast Alabama young adults in their early-to-mid 20s. None have legal means to fancy gear or designer clothing. What signals archetype is small wardrobe and behavioral cues — not costume.

---

### Adam / Amanda — Artificer

**Identity:** PC slot, Artificer archetype. M/F pair: Adam / Amanda. Has not yet appeared in canonical prose.

**Personality:** Practical, hands-on, problem-solvers. Quiet competence — they'd rather build than talk. The friends who can fix anything with what's on hand. Patient with broken things; less patient with people who break their patience. Grease under the nails. Hands that have built and rebuilt the same engines for years.

**Story function:** Mechanical capability for the crew. Vehicle repairs, gear-fixing, lock-picking through brute force or finesse. If the campaign needs a workaround, they engineer it. Often the crew member who comes back with "I can make that work."

**Voice/manner:** Few words. Longer pauses than most. Descriptive when explaining technical things; otherwise minimal. Often says "give me a minute" when handed a problem.

**Branding cues:** Coveralls or work-shirts, jeans, scuffed boots. Tool clipped to belt. Hands always visible — they tell the character's story. Iconography: wrench, oil-stained rag, tool roll. Visual register is "the friend whose hands you trust because they've fixed things for you."

---

### Benson / Brianna — Barbarian

**Identity:** PC slot, Barbarian archetype. M/F pair: Benson / Brianna. Has not yet appeared in canonical prose.

**Personality:** Direct, physical, emotional in the open. Not stupid — strong is a discipline they've cultivated. Loyal in a way that's almost tribal. Slow to anger but decisive when it lands. Built from work, not the gym. Doesn't perform toughness because they don't need to.

**Story function:** Physical capability for the crew. When a problem needs to be carried, broken, or stood against, they handle it. Pairs well with the more strategic archetypes — they execute the heavy parts of someone else's plan, or improvise when plans fall apart.

**Voice/manner:** Loud when comfortable, quiet when not. Curses without thinking. Uses "we" and "us" more than "I" and "me."

**Branding cues:** Tank tops or t-shirts, jeans or sweatpants, work boots. Tattoos and small scars visible. A built-but-not-toned body — strength from labor, not vanity. Iconography: tape on knuckles, a beat-up duffel bag, a hand-rolled cigarette. Visual register is "the friend you call when you can't get out of the bar fight by talking."

---

### Byron / Beth — Bard

**Identity:** PC slot, Bard archetype. M/F pair: Byron / Beth. **Beth** is the canonically rendered version in prose — the crew's social glue, fronts the tour-agent scheme in Ep 8.

**Personality:** Bright, warm, performative in a way that's mostly genuine. Knows how to be the version of herself a given room needs. Real underneath; the performance is a tool, not a mask. Reads people for what they want to hear and gives it to them — not maliciously, just because she's good at it. The crew's diplomat.

**Story function:** Fronts the tour-agent scheme. Talks the crew through fights. Brings food to morning-after gatherings. When the crew needs charm-work or social engineering, Beth is the one. May also be the one who breaks — the brightest light burns out hardest if the campaign goes long.

**Voice/manner:** Honey-thick when she's on, casual and observant when she's off. Knows the difference between her stage voice and her real voice and uses both deliberately.

**Branding cues:** Carefully chosen wardrobe — looks effortless, isn't. Iconography: phone-as-camera, lipstick, a brand-friendly smile. Visual register is "the friend who could absolutely make this work as a career if circumstances were different."

---

### Christian / Carla — Cleric

**Identity:** PC slot, Cleric archetype. M/F pair: Christian / Carla. **Carla** is the canonically rendered version in prose — the harm-reduction clinic worker who names Ted in Ep 6.

**Personality:** Plainspoken, clinic-tired, has seen too much and doesn't perform it. Operates from quiet competence, not heroism. Believes in harm reduction because she's watched abstinence fail. Sees the dealers because they sell on the corner by her clinic. Knows things she doesn't share unless asked directly.

**Story function:** The bridge between Alex's world and the consequence-world. As a clinic worker, she sees what Glitter does to people — and she's still Alex's friend. Names Ted in Ep 6. Functions as the crew's moral conscience without ever moralizing — her presence is the question the crew is failing to ask itself.

**Voice/manner:** Quiet, flat, exhausted-but-not-bitter. "I've seen him selling near the clinic" rather than "I think you should consider…" — she states facts, lets people decide.

**Branding cues:** Clinic scrubs (pale blue), often still in them when she shows up to gatherings. Comfortable shoes. Iconography: the scrubs, a small clipboard or clinic ID badge. Visual register is "the friend who has the job that means she sees the version of this story that hasn't happened to you yet."

---

### Dave / Diana — Druid (techno-nerd)

**Identity:** PC slot, Druid archetype rendered as techno-nerd (not nature-mystic). M/F pair: Dave / Diana. **Diana** is the canonically rendered version in prose — the crew's tech support.

**Personality:** Quiet, focused, present through her devices. Not antisocial — just always doing three things at once and one of them is on a screen. Reads the room through what people post, what's on their phones, what's in the public record. Mistakes are rare; when she makes one, it's because she trusted the wrong data source.

**Story function:** Tech support for the crew. Builds the fake landing pages for the tour-agent scheme, monitors police scanners, runs background checks on people the crew is about to deal with. Functions as the crew's eyes outside the room.

**Voice/manner:** Speaks while looking at her screen. Sentences in fragments because she's thinking about other things simultaneously. Quick to confirm when she has data, slow to confirm when she doesn't.

**Branding cues:** Tablet or device always in hand. Comfortable clothes — hoodie, joggers, headphones around her neck. Earth tones in clothing (the only nature-link in the Druid-as-techno render). Iconography: the tablet, headphones, an open laptop. Visual register is "the friend you DM at 3 AM who responds immediately because she was already up."

### Frank / Fiona — Fighter

**Identity:** PC slot, Fighter archetype. M/F pair: Frank / Fiona. Has not yet appeared in canonical prose.

**Personality:** Disciplined the way Barbarians aren't. Trained. Reads fights before they happen. Has been the person at the gym since high school and uses that competence as identity. Slight chip-on-shoulder energy — knows they're tough and won't apologize for it. Different from Barbarians: where Benson/Brianna break things, Frank/Fiona break them *with purpose*.

**Story function:** Combat capability with strategic thinking. Often the crew member who handles direct confrontation without escalating it past what was needed. Pairs naturally with the more cerebral archetypes — Frank/Fiona executes, Diana or William/Whitney plans.

**Voice/manner:** Clipped, observant. Asks setup questions before committing. "Where are their hands?" before "What's the play?" Doesn't waste breath.

**Branding cues:** Tank tops, sweatpants, taped knuckles or wrist wraps. Gym bag often nearby. Athletic but functional, not cut. Iconography: heavy bag, hand wraps, mouthguard in a pocket. Visual register is "the friend who has been training for a fight that may or may not ever come."

### Mike / Mikaela — Monk

**Identity:** PC slot, Monk archetype. M/F pair: Mike / Mikaela. Has not yet appeared in canonical prose.

**Personality:** Quiet self-control in a working-class register. May or may not have actual martial-arts training; what matters is the practiced calm. Doesn't get drawn into fights or drama. Watches first. Speaks once. Loyal but not demonstrative. The friend whose presence calms a room without anyone naming why.

**Story function:** De-escalation and observation. The crew member who reads a room and acts last. Often arrives at decisions independently and reveals them only at the moment of execution. A candidate for the "person who notices what everyone else missed" beat late-act.

**Voice/manner:** Speaks rarely. When they do, complete sentences without filler. Pauses before responding to direct questions.

**Branding cues:** Simple t-shirts, comfortable pants, well-worn sneakers. No watch. Hair short or pulled back simply. Iconography: a single notebook, plain water bottle, no phone visible. Visual register is "the friend you forget is in the room until they speak, and then everyone listens."

### Philip / Prim — Paladin

**Identity:** PC slot, Paladin archetype. M/F pair: Philip / Prim. Has not yet appeared in canonical prose.

**Personality:** Rules-oriented. Believes in fairness, in commitments kept, in showing up on time. Reads as ROTC or veteran — military-bearing without necessarily being military. Tension with the crew's illegal work is *always present* — they're doing this because it's Alex, not because they approve. The most likely to push back on a plan that crosses their line.

**Story function:** Moral counterweight inside the crew. When the crew is about to do something that has lasting consequences, Philip or Prim names the cost. The dissenting voice. Stays anyway because of loyalty. Their breaking point — if they hit it — is a major late-act beat.

**Voice/manner:** Direct, complete sentences, dislikes ambiguity. Will literally ask "Is that legal?" knowing the answer. Calls people by their full first name.

**Branding cues:** Button-up shirts or polos, dark jeans, clean shoes. Hair neat. The most put-together of the crew. Iconography: a military-style accessory (a coin, a watch, a small pin — distinct from Alex's pendant), an unlit cigarette they never smoke. Visual register is "the friend who would be a cop if life had gone slightly differently."

### Rick / Rose — Ranger

**Identity:** PC slot, Ranger archetype. M/F pair: Rick / Rose. Has not yet appeared in canonical prose.

**Personality:** Patient. Quiet. Knows the woods around Blueville, the back roads, the spots where deer cross. Spends time alone by choice. Trusts animals more than people but doesn't perform it. Tracks the world through small details others miss.

**Story function:** Reconnaissance, navigation, escape routes. If the crew needs to disappear for a few days, Rick or Rose knows where to go. The crew's wilderness/back-country specialist. Strong candidate for the role of "person who gets Alex out of town" in a Pyrrhic-save ending.

**Voice/manner:** Soft-spoken. Speaks slowly. Uses spatial language ("northeast of the lake," "past the second turn"). Gets quieter under stress, not louder.

**Branding cues:** Flannel shirts, cargo pants, scuffed hiking boots. A small worn backpack. Hair shoulder-length or pulled back. Iconography: compass, pocket knife (legal length), a folded map. Visual register is "the friend who showed up to your wedding in boots and a flannel and looked the most comfortable in the room."

### Rob / Roni — Rogue

**Identity:** PC slot, Rogue archetype. M/F pair: Rob / Roni. **Roni** is the canonically rendered version in prose — the crew's career sneak.

**Personality:** Procedural, practical, low-drama. Asks the right questions in the right order — "Exactly how many vials? Did they see the labels? Did they say where they were from?" Gets things done because she knows how to read a situation. Has done minor crime since she was a teenager; this is not her first rodeo, just her highest-stakes one. Loyal to Alex through long association, not sentiment.

**Story function:** Mechanical engine of the crew. Handles scouting, theft, watch duty. Reads people the way Becky reads Alex. In the campaign loop, she's often the one who proposes the plan and the one who executes it. If something goes sideways, she's calmest.

**Voice/manner:** Short, declarative. No filler. Confirms with single words. ("Right." "Got it." "Where?")

**Branding cues:** Practical wardrobe — fitted dark clothes, soft-soled shoes, hair tied back. Iconography: a folding knife, a scout's posture, a small backpack. Visual register is "the friend who already cased the place before you got there."

### Sam / Sarah — Sorcerer

**Identity:** PC slot, Sorcerer archetype. M/F pair: Sam / Sarah. **Sam** is the canonically rendered version in prose — appears as nonbinary they/them across Eps 6 and 8. In the group portrait, **Sam is rendered male** per the canonical visual decision.

**Personality:** Intuitive, observant, quiet. Watches before speaking, then doesn't speak. Notices things other people miss because they're not trying to fit in. The crew's silent witness. Has opinions but rarely volunteers them; when asked directly, gives precise, specific answers. Loves Alex without needing to say so.

**Story function:** Stays behind during the gig (Ep 8) — "someone needs to hold down the home base." Functions as the connective tissue of the crew when others are away. Late-act, may be the one who notices something everyone else has missed.

**Voice/manner:** Brief. Often silent. When they do speak, the room listens because Sam doesn't waste words.

**Branding cues:** Quiet wardrobe — earth tones, layers, soft fabrics. Hair in their face, often. Iconography: a small notebook, a thermos, a book they don't fully read. Visual register is "the friend you forgot was in the room until they say something that recontextualizes everything."

**Note on Sam's gender canon:** Prose canon renders Sam as nonbinary they/them. Visual canon (group portrait) renders Sam as male. Players picking the Sorcerer slot may choose Sam (M), Sam (NB), or Sarah (F). The canonical telling uses Sam-NB. The Designer should treat these as compatible — same character, multiple presentations across media.

### William / Whitney — Wizard

**Identity:** PC slot, Wizard archetype. M/F pair: William / Whitney. Has not yet appeared in canonical prose.

**Personality:** Curious by default. The friend who already read about the thing before it came up. Different from Diana (Druid-techno) in that William/Whitney is interested in *systems and history* — laws, processes, how institutions work — where Diana is interested in *data and access*. The crew member who knows the actual statute being violated.

**Story function:** Research, planning, exposition. Often the one who explains how a system works so the crew can find the gap. Pairs with Diana on tech — William/Whitney handles the analog side: paperwork, public records, archive-digging.

**Voice/manner:** Speaks in qualified statements. Uses "actually" and "technically" and means them. Will correct misinformation patiently.

**Branding cues:** Sweater or cardigan over a t-shirt, glasses (one or both). A worn paperback always somewhere on their person. Iconography: a stack of paperbacks, a thrifted briefcase, ballpoint pens. Visual register is "the friend who reads the contract before they sign it and tells you what to push back on."

## Branding throughline notes

**The throughline:** *Everyone of consequence peaked in high school.* This should be felt in every character's wardrobe and visual identity — even the young characters dress like they're trying to be someone they were three years ago or someone they think they might have been if things had gone differently. Blueville's visual identity is the visible drag of a place that has already had its best year.

**Three accent colors, sparingly:**
- **Yellow #ffff00** — pilgrim/presidential signage, Glitter packaging, Smoke Shop exterior. The color of attention without warmth.
- **Blue #0088ff** — Smoke Shop OPEN sign, Glitter visual effect, currency, laser weapon emitter. The color of the trade.
- **Red #ff4444** — Smoke Shop awning, the cash band, danger signage near the warehouses. The color of arrival/consequence.

These are not character colors. They are *world* colors. Characters are rendered in the muted Gulf Coast register; the accents punctuate the world they move through.

**For frontend / UI / prints specifically:**

If the Designer is building a website, a player handout, a campaign reference card, or a promotional asset — the lead aesthetic is **"illustrated zine for a story that hurts to read."** Hand-feel rather than design-system-feel. Typewriter or hand-lettered headers. Photo-collage textures where photos appear. The visual identity should never look slick; if it ever reads as "polished," it's lost the project.
